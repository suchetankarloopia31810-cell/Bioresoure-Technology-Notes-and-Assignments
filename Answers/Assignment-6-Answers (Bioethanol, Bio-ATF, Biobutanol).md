## Q1. Write a brief note on bioethanol and discuss the process for 2nd-generation bioethanol production.

**Bioethanol** is ethyl alcohol (C₂H₅OH) obtained from **bioresources (biomass)** by **hydrolysis or sugar fermentation**. It is a principal **petrol substitute** for road vehicles. (Ethanol made chemically from ethylene + steam is *not* bioethanol because the source is non-renewable.) It is a clear, colourless, biodegradable, low-toxicity liquid; a **high-octane fuel** that has replaced lead as an octane enhancer. Blending ethanol with petrol oxygenates the fuel → more complete combustion, fewer emissions. Common blend **E10** (10% ethanol) needs no engine modification; **E85** (85% ethanol) needs flexible-fuel vehicles.

**2nd-generation (2G) bioethanol process** (from non-food cellulosic biomass — switchgrass, miscanthus, woodchips, corn stover, bagasse, sawdust):
- Cellulosic biomass = **cellulose + hemicellulose + lignin**; lignin acts as a protective barrier hindering depolymerization.
- Process is complex: biomass is first **pretreated** (chemically or enzymatically) to break the polymeric units and expose C5–C6 sugars → **hydrolysis** to fermentable sugars → **microbial fermentation** to ethanol → distillation/dehydration to anhydrous ethanol.
- Conversion can be **direct fermentation** (hydrolysis–fermentation) or **indirect fermentation** (syngas fermentation).
- 2G plants commissioned in 2014 (POET-DSM, Abengoa) at 25 million gallons/year; sustainability depends on feedstock availability at reasonable prices.

## Q2. Briefly explain the global scenario of biofuel production.

- **USA and Brazil are the world leaders** in bioethanol production; India ranks poorly (large energy importer).
- Global biofuel supply rose sharply from 2000 to 2010 (driven by blending mandates).
- For 2013–2015, the supply was about **3:1 ethanol to biodiesel**; conventional biofuels (sugar, starch, vegetable oil, animal fat) dominate.
- Advanced biofuels (2G/3G/4G) are expected to grow to **124 billion litres per year by 2030** (International Renewable Energy Agency).
- World bioethanol production and consumption are predicted to keep rising toward 2024; consumption has outgrown production for several consecutive years due to fuel-ethanol blending demand.

## Q3. Classify and describe bioethanol based on feedstocks.

Based on source of raw material:
- **First Generation (1G):** from **food-based** feedstocks — starch/sugar (sugarcane, sugar beet, sweet sorghum, corn, wheat). Sugars fermented directly; starch hydrolyzed (α-amylase + glucoamylase) to glucose then fermented. Criticized for food competition and land-use change.
- **Second Generation (2G):** from **non-food** feedstocks — lignocellulosic biomass (energy crops, agricultural & wood residues). Requires pretreatment; lower GHG emissions; needs more capital and sophisticated equipment.
- **Third Generation (3G):** from **algal biomass** (micro/macroalgae). Grows on non-arable land, uses CO₂ from flue gases, needs no fresh water; obstacle is process economics (harvesting is 20–30% of cost).
- **Fourth Generation (4G):** from **captured CO₂** via advanced technologies (electrochemical synthesis, oxide electrolysis, petroleum hydroprocessing); considered **carbon negative**; still embryonic.

## Q4. Write a short note on the important feedstocks suitable for bioethanol production.

The basic materials should have high carbon & hydrogen and low oxygen/nitrogen content. Important feedstocks:
- **Alfalfa** (*Medicago sativa*) — no N fertilizer needed; low glucose yield (low-efficiency cellulose hydrolysis); stems rich in crude protein.
- **Switchgrass** (*Panicum virgatum*) — perennial, US; ethanol yield ≈ wheat; C4 plant; adapts to lean soil.
- **Sweet sorghum** — starch-rich grains, high-saccharose stems, lignocellulose leaves/bagasse; tolerates drought/flooding/alkalinity.
- **Cassava** — tuber; must be processed 3–4 days after harvest.
- **Spruce / Willow** — lignocellulosic woody materials (willow gives ~79% ethanol yield with H₂SO₄ pretreatment + SSF).
- **Reed canary grass; Sugarcane** (12–17% sugar, bagasse for energy); **Sugar beet**.
- **Cereals** — wheat, barley, winter rye, corn stover (must leave 30% on ground to prevent erosion).
- **Jerusalem artichoke** — tubers rich in inulin.
- **Biomass wastes/residues** — agricultural (straw, husk, bagasse), forest (bark, sawdust), industrial (brewer's spent grain), MSW (food waste, paper), whey, crude glycerol.

## Q5. Discuss the common flow diagram for bioethanol production from 1st- and 2nd-generation feedstocks.

*(Reproduce Figure-31: common flow diagram.)*
- Fermentation of **simple sugars** by microorganisms (yeast) is used instead of chemical synthesis.
- **Sugar feedstock** (sugarcane, molasses, beet, sweet sorghum): yeast consumes simple sugars **directly** → ethanol.
- **Starch feedstock** (1G): starch must be **depolymerized to glucose** before fermentation. Two-stage enzymatic hydrolysis:
  1. **Liquefaction** — gelatinized starch hydrolyzed by **α-amylase** (cuts α-1,4) → drop in viscosity → dextrins.
  2. **Saccharification** — dextrins hydrolyzed by **glucoamylase** (cuts α-1,4 and α-1,6) → glucose.
  Glucose then fermented by yeast → ethanol.
- **Cellulosic feedstock** (2G): needs **pretreatment + hydrolysis** to release sugars (more difficult/costly than starch).
- After fermentation, the "beer" (~10% v/v ethanol) is **distilled and dehydrated** → anhydrous ethanol.

## Q6. Explain the simplified scheme for bioethanol production from sugarcane.

*(Reproduce Figures-33/34.)* Sugarcane is the most efficient raw material (low fossil energy use; bagasse provides process energy). Steps:
1. **Cleaning** — wet or dry (dry is more eco-friendly, no water waste/sucrose loss).
2. **Grinding & juice extraction** — crush fibre (density 175 → 450 kg/m³); crusher/mill (4–7 mill suits) with hot water (~70 °C); remaining **bagasse** (50–52% humidity) burnt in boilers for steam/electricity.
3. **Clarification** — juice sieved, treated with sulfate/liming, heated to **105 °C** (lowers microbial contamination, coagulates colloids); sludge used as fertilizer. Concentration/crystallization gives sugar; residual = **molasses** (glucose 5–20%, sucrose 45–60%).
4. **Fermentation** — must (molasses + juice, 18–22% reducing sugars) + yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*). Enzymes **sucrase (invertase) and zymase** convert sugars:
   - C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + C₆H₁₂O₆
   - C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂
   Types: batch, **fed-batch** (used in ~75% of Brazilian mills), continuous.
5. **Recovery** — broth ("wine," 6–12% v/v alcohol); yeast separated by centrifugation (recycled after acid treatment); wine **distilled** → hydrated ethanol (96° GL), by-product **vinasse/stillage** (used as fertilizer). **Dehydration** (cyclohexane or molecular sieves) → anhydrous ethanol.

## Q7. Make a proper comparison of specifications between gasoline and bioethanol.

*(Reproduce Table-2: specifications.)* Key points:
- **Energy content:** bioethanol has much **lower energy content** (~two-thirds of gasoline on a volume basis) → reduced vehicle range per tank.
- **Octane number:** ethanol's octane is **higher** → better antiknock; allows higher compression ratio → higher efficiency.
- **Oxygen content:** ethanol contains oxygen → cleaner, more complete, lower-temperature combustion.
- **Reid vapour pressure:** very **low** for ethanol → slow evaporation (low evaporative emissions, lower explosion risk) **but** poor cold-start (engines hard to start below 20 °C with pure ethanol).
- **Use:** blended with gasoline (E5, E10) without engine change; E85 needs flexible-fuel vehicles; also used as **ETBE** (45% bioethanol + 55% isobutylene) octane enhancer (preferred over carcinogenic MTBE).
- **Problems:** corrosive to fuel injector/pump (hygroscopic); cold-start issues; emulsion formation (bioethanol–water–oil) can cause engine failure.

## Q8. Discuss India's status in bioethanol production, consumption and requirement.

- India has ~**330 distilleries** producing > **4.8 billion litres** of rectified spirit/year; ~166 can distill ~**2.6 billion litres** of ethanol. Feedstocks: C & B heavy molasses, sugarcane juice, damaged food grains.
- **Consumption (2019):** forecast ~**3.8 billion litres** (up 22%); growth ~14%/yr (5-yr avg) driven by fuel-ethanol blending; consumption has outgrown production for 5 consecutive years.
- **Production (2019):** ~**3 billion litres** (11% rise); from C-heavy molasses (~1.8 bn L), B-heavy molasses, damaged food grains, sugarcane juice.
- **Blending:** ~6.6% achievable if all molasses-ethanol blended; expected national average ~5.8% in 2019 (4.1% in 2018). **Ethanol Blended Petrol (EBP) programme** launched January 2003 to promote eco-friendly fuel and reduce import dependency.
- **Imports:** India remains a **net importer**; **USA is the single largest supplier** (94% share); 2019 imports up to ~750 million litres. Other suppliers: Pakistan, South Africa, UAE, UK.
- India is pushing toward a **"Methanol Economy"** and net-zero petroleum imports.

## Q9. Write a brief note on bio-aviation turbine fuel and highlight its significance w.r.t. its necessity.

**Bio Aviation Turbine Fuel (Bio-ATF)** is a biofuel for aircraft, prepared from **animal fat, vegetable oil and agricultural waste**; a renewable substitute for petroleum jet fuel. Jet fuel powers gas-turbine engines and has stricter quality requirements than road fuels. About 4 of every 42-gallon barrel of crude makes jet fuel; the aviation industry uses ~1.5–1.7 billion barrels/year. Fuel is the largest operating cost in aviation.

**Significance / Necessity:**
- Reduces **dependency on a single energy source** and avoids petroleum price volatility.
- Potential to **reduce CO₂/GHG emissions** over its life cycle (SAF from algae, jatropha, waste can cut the carbon footprint by up to **80%**).
- Aviation accounts for ~**12% of transport fuel** and ~**2% of GHG**; CO₂ from commercial flights rose 32% (2013–2018). To meet the **2015 Paris Accord** and aim for a **50% GHG reduction by 2050** (vs 2005), increased bio-jet use is the **only technically viable low-carbon means** for aviation (electricity/solar/hydrogen are not feasible for aircraft).
- **HEFA** and **Fischer–Tropsch** synthesis are the most promising technologies. (India: SpiceJet flew on Bio-ATF on Aug 7, 2018; IAF plans to expand biojet use.)

## Q10. Enlist the various production technologies used for Bio-ATF and give a diagram explaining the process-wise raw material and technology overview.

*(Reproduce Table-2 and Figure-6: process-wise raw material & technology overview.)* Representative production technologies:
1. **Alcohol-to-Jet (ATJ):** hydrolysis of wood-fibre biomass or glucose into intermediate alcohols (methanol, ethanol, butanol, fatty alcohols) → dehydration & oligomerization; divided into **ethanol-to-jet** and **butanol-to-jet**.
2. **Oil-to-Jet (OTJ):** from animal/plant tallow (waste vegetable oil, beef tallow, microalgae). Includes **HEFA** (hydrotreated esters and fatty acids), HRJ, catalytic hydrothermolysis (CH), rapid thermal decomposition (HDCJ).
3. **Gas-to-Jet (GTJ):** biogas/natural gas/syngas (from wood-fibre biomass) → bio-jet via gas fermentation or **Fischer–Tropsch** processes.
4. **Sugar-to-Jet (STJ):** catalytic upgrading/biological conversion of glucose/starch via direct sugar to hydrocarbons (DSHC) and catalytic upgrading.

As of 2016, **ASTM D7566** approves five processes: **FT-SPK, HEFA, SIP (synthesized iso-paraffins), ATJ** (and FT-SPK with aromatics). Bio-jet is also classified by pathway: **fermentation, deoxidation, or thermal decomposition.**

## Q11. How can biojet fuel be manufactured from bioethanol? Explain with a simplified flow diagram.

Via the **Alcohol-to-Jet (ATJ)** process using bioethanol. *(Reproduce Figure-8: technical overview of ATJ.)* High-purity **anhydrous ethanol (99.5–99.9%)** is used. Steps:
1. **Dehydration:** oxygen content of ethanol is removed by catalytic dehydration (catalysts: alumina, transition-metal oxides, zeolite) → **ethylene**.
2. **Oligomerization (polymerization):** ethylene converted to **alpha-olefins** (C4–C20, ~96–97% yield) to build up carbon distribution (catalysts: Ziegler-Natta, homogeneous chromium-diphosphine, heterogeneous zeolite). A wide carbon range (5% C4, 50% C6–C10, 30% C12–C14, 12% C16–C18) is obtained.
3. **Selective distillation:** separates light oil and aviation-fuel fractions; low-carbon C4–C8 hydrocarbons are **recycled** to oligomerization.
4. **Hydrogenation (upgrading):** olefins hydrogenated in H₂ atmosphere (controlled T, P, flow) → saturated hydrocarbons matching jet fuel (C6–C16); excess H₂ reused, un-hydrogenated hydrocarbons recycled.
5. **Blending:** the hydrogenated product is **blended with conventional ATF** to give Bio-ATF.

## Q12. What is biobutanol? Discuss its significance as a biofuel.

**Biobutanol** is a **4-carbon alcohol (butyl alcohol, C₄H₉OH)** produced from the same feedstocks as bioethanol (corn grain and other biomass) by **microbial fermentation**. It is colourless, flammable, miscible with ethanol/ether/organic solvents. Used as a solvent, in cosmetics, hydraulic fluids, detergents, drugs, antibiotics, vitamins, and as a chemical intermediate. Carbon atoms can form straight or branched chains → isomers (n-butanol, 2-butanol, iso-butanol, tert-butanol); only **n-butanol** (straight-chain) is produced from biomass.

**Significance as a biofuel (advantages over ethanol):**
- **Higher energy content and burning efficiency** (longer driving distance); air-fuel ratio and energy content **closer to gasoline** → can be blended with gasoline in **any proportion**.
- **Less volatile/explosive**, higher flash point, lower vapour pressure → safer to handle; can be **shipped through existing fuel pipelines**.
- Can be used **directly or blended** with gasoline/diesel **without vehicle retrofit**.
- Less corrosive, high hydrophobicity, high viscosity → overcomes ethanol's drawbacks (low heating value, corrosive acetic acid, hygroscopicity, phase separation, difficult storage/transport).
- Now seen as a potential fuel additive or direct replacement for gasoline/diesel. (David Ramey drove on 100% butanol; DuPont and BP financed development.)

## Q13. Tabulate the structures, properties and main applications of N-butanol, 2-butanol, iso-butanol and tert-butanol.

*(Reproduce Table-1.)* All are isomers of C₄H₉OH, differing by the position of the –OH group and carbon-chain structure:

| Isomer | Structure | Key properties | Main applications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **n-Butanol** | Straight chain CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH; –OH on terminal C | Higher boiling point/viscosity; **only isomer producible from biomass** | Solvent, gasoline additive/fuel, industrial cleaner, chemical intermediate |
| **2-Butanol** (sec-butanol) | –OH on a secondary (internal) carbon | Lower boiling point than n-butanol | Solvent, industrial cleaner, gasoline additive |
| **Iso-butanol** | Branched (CH₃)₂CHCH₂OH | Branched-chain properties | Solvent, gasoline additive, industrial cleaner |
| **Tert-butanol** | Branched (CH₃)₃COH; –OH on tertiary carbon | Highest octane number among isomers | Solvent, gasoline additive (octane booster) |

Though octane number, boiling point and viscosity differ, the **main applications are similar** (solvents, industrial cleaners, gasoline additives). All can be made from fossil fuels; only n-butanol from biomass.

## Q14. Compare the properties of butanol and other fuels.

*(Reproduce Table-3: properties of butanol and other fuels.)* Compared with **ethanol and gasoline**:
- **Energy content:** butanol > ethanol, and **close to gasoline** (ethanol's heating value is much lower than gasoline → higher fuel consumption if engine not retrofitted).
- **Air–fuel ratio:** butanol close to gasoline → easy blending in any proportion.
- **Corrosiveness:** butanol **less corrosive** (ethanol produces corrosive acetic acid on burning; a preservative must be added when ethanol > 15%).
- **Hygroscopicity:** ethanol is hygroscopic → phase separation with high water content; butanol is more **hydrophobic**.
- **Volatility / vapour pressure:** butanol has **lower volatility, higher flash point, lower vapour pressure** → safer to handle and transportable via existing pipelines.
- **Storage/transport:** butanol easier than ethanol; usable directly or blended without vehicle retrofit.

## Q15. Draw a representative schematic diagram for fermentative production of butanol from lignocellulosic biomass.

*(Reproduce Figure-1 and Figure-2.)* Three major steps: **pretreatment → detoxification → fermentation** (plus strain development and process integration).

1. **Pretreatment:** opens the lignocellulosic structure (cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin) to release sugars. Methods:
   - **Physical:** milling/grinding, extrusion, microwave, ultrasonication.
   - **Physicochemical:** steam explosion, steam treatment, hydrothermolysis, ammonia fibre expansion, hot-water treatment.
   - **Chemical:** alkali, acid, ozonolysis, ionic liquid, organosolv.
   - **Enzymatic hydrolysis:** degrades cellulose/xylan to C6/C5 sugar monomers.
2. **Detoxification:** removes inhibitors formed during pretreatment (furfural, HMF, formic/acetic/levulinic acids, phenolics) by electrodialysis, liming/overliming, activated carbon/charcoal, dilution, resin treatment, filtration/centrifugation. (Note: furfural/HMF/acetic acid are not strongly inhibitory to clostridia, sometimes stimulatory; formic acid is inhibitory.)
3. **Fermentation (ABE):** **biphasic** — first **acidogenesis** (acetic & butyric acids), then acids re-assimilated to solvents **acetone, butanol, ethanol** by *Clostridium* species. Modes: batch (simple, low contamination), fed-batch (tackles substrate inhibition), continuous/chemostat (higher productivity). In-situ product removal (gas stripping, pervaporation, adsorption) alleviates butanol toxicity.

## Q16. Discuss the challenges and their solutions with respect to butanol production.

*(Reproduce Table-5: challenges and solutions.)* Major challenges and solutions for sustainable, economical fermentative butanol production:
- **High substrate cost** → use cheap/free feedstock (lignocellulosic wastes — wheat straw, corn core, switchgrass) and cost-efficient processing.
- **Solvent (butanol) toxicity** — limits titer to ~2% → in-situ product removal (gas stripping, pervaporation, adsorption, extraction, pertraction, reverse osmosis, membrane distillation); metabolic engineering/mutagenesis for **butanol-tolerant strains**.
- **Low cell density / low productivity** → cell immobilization, cell recycling, biofilm reactors; fed-batch and continuous modes.
- **By-product formation (acetone, ethanol) lowering selectivity** → metabolic engineering to disrupt acetone pathways, homo-butanol fermentation, conversion of acetone to isopropanol.
- **Inefficient substrate utilization** → disrupt Carbon Catabolite Repression; overexpress xylose-transport and catalytic-enzyme genes.
- **High downstream cost** → efficient separation; process integration & intensification (SSF/SSCF, consolidated bioprocessing CBP).

## Q17. Write a brief note on the global scenario of the bio-butanol market — growth, trends and forecast (2019–2024).

- The bio-butanol market is expected to register a **CAGR of ~8.36%** during 2019–2024.
- **Drivers:** reduction of carbon emissions, growing prominence as a **building block for chemical manufacturing**, and superiority as an automobile fuel over bioethanol (high energy content, less corrosive, blendable at any concentration, low vapour pressure/volatility, fewer emissions). Growing number of cars (expected to rise to ~2 billion by 2050) boosts motor-fuel demand. USA and Europe have begun fleet-testing.
- **Asia-Pacific to dominate**, with **China** holding the largest share (largest producer of paints/coatings and adhesives; rapid urbanization — 60% target by 2020; growing construction/automotive industries).
- **Competitive landscape:** market is in a **nascent, consolidated** stage. **Cathay Industrial Biotech** (corn-based n-butanol, Jilin, China) claimed to be the world's largest producer (2017). Potential entrants: Microvi Biotechnologies, Cobalt Technologies, ButaNext.
- *Historical note:* first lab synthesis by **Pasteur (1861/1862)**; industrial **ABE fermentation** began ~1912–1916 (Weizmann process, for acetone in WWI); revived after the 1970s oil crisis with advances in metabolic engineering.
