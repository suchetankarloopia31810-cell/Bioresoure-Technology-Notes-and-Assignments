## Q1. What is Single Cell Protein (SCP) and why is there a need for it? Discuss its advantages and disadvantages.

**Definition:** Single Cell Protein (SCP) refers to the **dead, dried cells of microorganisms** — such as yeast, fungi, bacteria and algae — that are grown on various carbon sources for their protein content. The term was first used by **Carol Wilson in 1967**, replacing less aesthetic terms like "petro protein" and "microbial protein." Because most of these microorganisms are unicellular, the protein obtained from them is called single cell protein. SCP also contains vitamins, fats and minerals. Yeast is most often chosen due to its superior nutritional quality and value as an animal-feed supplement.

**Need for SCP:**
- It is an idea to **solve global food/protein scarcity**, especially the protein shortage of Third World nations.
- The agriculture sector uses large areas for protein crops but gives **low production per unit area**; SCP can relieve this pressure.
- It is estimated that SCP fermenters covering **one-third of a square mile can supply 10% of the world's protein requirement**.
- Agriculture is affected by **climate**, whereas SCP technology is **independent of climate** and works round the year.
- The scenario for animal protein is also not encouraging.

**Advantages:** high protein content; contains all essential amino acids; some microorganisms rich in vitamins; high surface-area-to-volume ratio; small doubling time and high growth rate; flexibility in substrate use; independence of cultivable land and climate; works on a continuous basis; eco-friendly; cost-effective; energy efficient; can be genetically controlled.

**Disadvantages / Drawbacks:**
- **High nucleic acid content** (18–25 g/100 g protein dry weight) → digestion produces purines → elevated **uric acid** → gout and kidney stones.
- High risk of **contamination** during production and difficulty in **recovering cells**.
- Microbial **cell wall may be indigestible**.
- **Intolerable colour and flavour** (especially in algae); possible skin reactions and gastrointestinal reactions (nausea, vomiting) with yeast.
- Algal SCP rich in chlorophyll may be unsuitable for humans (except *Spirulina*).
- Low density (1–2 g dry weight/litre of substrate).
- Filamentous fungi grow slowly and some strains produce **mycotoxins**, requiring careful screening.

## Q2. What are the various suitable substrates for SCP production? Discuss the sources of microorganisms and the selection criteria.

**Suitable substrates:**
- **Algae:** carbon dioxide (CO₂) and sunlight are the main growth sources.
- **Fungi:** sulfite waste liquor, prawn shell wastes, dairy waste/whey, molasses; lignocellulosic wastes for specific fungi.
- **Bacteria:** C1–C4 compounds; agricultural, animal and food-processing wastes; methane, methanol.

**Sources of microorganisms:** algae, fungi, protozoa and bacteria.
- Among **algae**, *Spirulina* is used most extensively; biomass from *Chlorella, Scenedesmus, Dunaliella* is used on large scale (main problem: foul odour and tastelessness).
- **Fungi** such as *Aspergillus, Fusarium, Candida, Chaetomium, Trichoderma, Penicillium* — good due to wide substrate range and ability to withstand abiotic conditions.
- **Bacteria** such as *Bacillus, Lactobacillus, Pseudomonas, Aeromonas* — success not so encouraging.
- **Mixed cultures** show better stability and resistance to contamination.

**Selection criteria:**
- Ability to utilize carbon and nitrogen sources
- Moderate growth conditions
- Tolerance to pH, temperature and mineral concentrations
- Resistance against viral infection
- Non-toxicity and non-pathogenicity
- Acceptable nutritive value of cell mass

## Q3. Write a brief note on the nutritional aspects of SCP.

- Nutritional values vary with the microorganism and substrate used.
- Important assessment parameters: amino acid, protein, carbohydrate and lipid profiles, minerals and vitamins; palatability, allergies and gastrointestinal effects; long-term feeding trials, toxicological and carcinogenesis effects.
- Standard nutritional indices: **Protein Efficiency Ratio (PER)** = weight gained by microorganisms (g) / protein intake by microorganisms (g); **Biological Value (BV)** = retained nitrogen / absorbed nitrogen; **Net Protein Utilization (NPU)** = (retained nitrogen / nitrogen intake) × 100; **Protein Digestibility Value (PDV)**.
- **Algae:** rich in proteins (40–60%), fats (5–20%), vitamins A, B, C, D, E; minerals (7%), nucleic acid (4–6%).
- **Fungi:** contain B-complex vitamins; amino acid content reasonably high.
- **Yeast:** ~50% proteins, 20% non-proteins; contain lysine, thiamine, biotin, riboflavin, niacin, folic acid but **deficient in methionine**.
- *Aspergillus niger* is well balanced and at par with WHO standards.
- **Bacteria:** crude protein ~80% of dry weight.
- In general, SCP products contain ~**50–70% crude protein**.

## Q4. Explain the different factors which affect the production of SCP.

SCP production is complex and costly. Key factors:

1. **Selection of the kind of microorganism**, considering: growth rate, productivity and yields on the specific low-cost substrate; temperature and pH tolerance; oxygen requirements, heat generation and foaming characteristics; growth morphology and genetic stability; ease of SCP recovery and downstream processing; structure and composition of final product (protein content, amino acid profile, RNA level, flavour, aroma, colour, texture).
2. **Safety and acceptability** — most SCP is animal feed; products must meet safety requirements. Regulatory approval for human consumption is lengthy/expensive.
3. **Nucleic acid content** — naturally high because rapid-growth conditions raise RNA; digestion gives purines → uric acid → gout/kidney stones.
4. **Digestibility / allergenicity** — slow digestion of microbial cells; sensitivity/allergic reactions.
5. **Toxic/carcinogenic absorption** — e.g., polycyclic aromatic compounds from certain substrates.
6. **Economic aspects** — fermenter design (type), heat management, waste disposal, environmental concerns.

## Q5. Discuss single cell protein production processes along with a suitable flow diagram.

SCP is produced by **fermentation** using selected microbial strains multiplied on suitable raw materials, followed by separation. Process development begins with **microbial screening** (from soil, water, air or biological materials), then strain optimization (selection, mutation, genetic methods), determination of cultivation conditions and metabolic pathways, and finally process/apparatus engineering for large-scale production (with economic, safety, environmental and legal considerations).

**Cultivation methods:**
- **Submerged fermentation:** substrate is liquid containing nutrients; fermenter operated continuously; biomass harvested continuously then filtered/centrifuged and dried. Aeration is important; heat removed by cooling device. **Yeast and bacteria are recovered by centrifugation; filamentous fungi by filtration.**
- **Semisolid fermentation:** substrate used in a more solid state (e.g., cassava waste); uses a special bioreactor called the **U-loop fermentor** for mass and energy transfer.
- **Solid state fermentation (SSF):** solid substrate (rice/wheat bran) on flatbeds seeded with microorganisms, incubated in a temperature-controlled room for several days. Used for SCP, feeds, enzymes, ethanol, organic acids, B-complex vitamins, pigments, flavours.

**Main steps:** medium preparation → fermentation → separation → downstream processing. *(Reproduce Figure-1: Flow chart of single cell protein production.)*

## Q6. What are the various steps and technologies applied worldwide for SCP production? Discuss the Pruteen technology.

**Most important steps:**
1. **Selection of microorganism** — fast growing, wide substrate utilization, non-pathogenic, non-toxin producing, high quality & quantity of protein, limited nucleic acid.
2. **Selection of substratum** — cheap, readily utilizable, no toxic liberation.
3. **Growing conditions** — ambient, short incubation period, simple downstream process giving pure protein in minimum steps.
4. **Post-harvest step** — simple purification & drying without affecting protein quality; supplementation if protein is deficient.

**Technologies (processes) used worldwide:** Bel process, Pekilo process, Bioprotein process, **Pruteen process**, Quorn production, Symba process, Waterloo process, High-rate algal ponds.

**Pruteen Process:**
- Methanol-based processes were developed in Europe, the former Soviet Union, Japan and the USA, involving bacteria (*Hyphomicrobium, Methylococcus, Methylophilus, Methylotrophus*), yeasts and filamentous fungi.
- The most adventurous process was developed by **Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI), UK, in 1980**.
- It used the methylotrophic bacterium ***Methylophilus methylotrophus*** to produce a feed protein for chicken and pigs called **Pruteen**.
- It is the world's largest continuous aerobic bioprocess: a **3000 m³ pressure-cycle airlift fermenter** with inner loop and working fluid volume of 1.5 × 10⁶ L, capable of producing up to **50,000 tonnes of Pruteen protein**.

## Q7. Briefly describe the uses of SCP.

- Provides **instant energy**; good for healthy eyes and skin.
- Best **protein-supplemented food for undernourished children**; good source of vitamins, amino acids, minerals.
- **Therapeutic/natural medicine:** controlling obesity, lowering blood sugar in diabetics, reducing body weight, cholesterol and stress, preventing cholesterol accumulation.

## Q9. Write a brief note on antibiotics and discuss their top ten classes.

**Antibiotic** literally means "against life." It is a **chemical substance produced by a living organism (usually a microorganism) that is detrimental to other microorganisms**. They are commonly produced by soil microorganisms (bacteria and fungi) as a means of controlling competing microbes. Formally: *"compounds produced by bacteria and fungi which are capable of killing or inhibiting competing microbial species."*

- An antibiotic is an antimicrobial substance active against bacteria (antibacterial agent); it may **kill or inhibit** bacterial growth. A limited number have anti-protozoal activity.
- Antibiotics are **not effective against viruses** (cold, influenza) — those need **antivirals**.
- Before antibiotics, people died of minor infections (e.g., strep throat) and surgery was risky; after antibiotics became available in the **1940s**, life expectancy increased and surgeries became safer.
- **An ideal antibiotic:** kills/inhibits pathogens, causes no allergy to host cells, does not damage host cells, and is stable when stored in liquid or solid form.

**Top 10 classes of antibiotics:** (1) Penicillins (2) Tetracyclines (3) Cephalosporins (4) Quinolones (5) Lincomycins (6) Macrolides (7) Sulfonamides (8) Glycopeptides (9) Aminoglycosides (10) Carbapenems.

## Q10. Explain the different schemes for classification of antibiotics with suitable examples.

Antibiotics are classified by (1) chemical structure, (2) mechanism of action, (3) spectrum of activity, (4) mode of action, (5) absorbability.

- **By spectrum of activity:** *Narrow/short spectrum* — target only specific bacteria (e.g., penicillin G, vancomycin); *Broad spectrum* — act on many kinds of bacteria (e.g., tetracycline, chloramphenicol).
- **By mode of action:** *Bacteriostatic* — inhibit growth but do not kill (e.g., tetracycline); *Bactericidal* — kill bacteria (e.g., penicillin, cephalosporin).
- **By mechanism of action:** (1) inhibiting cell-wall synthesis (e.g., penicillin), (2) interfering with cell-membrane integrity, (3) inhibiting nucleic acid synthesis, (4) inhibiting protein synthesis, (5) inhibiting certain metabolic pathways.
- **By chemical structure:** beta-lactams, quinolones, sulphonamides, etc. **Beta-lactams** share a beta-lactam ring and have four sub-groups: **penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams.**

## Q11. Explain the chemical structure of antibiotics with the help of cephalosporin and penicillin.

- Both belong to the **beta-lactam** group (share a beta-lactam ring).
- **Cephalosporin:** general structure contains the **beta-lactam ring** with two significant side groups **R1 and R2**; by changing R1 and R2, various cephalosporins are obtained. *(Reproduce Figure-1: general structure of cephalosporin.)*
- **Penicillin:** basic structure is **6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA)** combined with an **N-acyl group** (side chain). It is a combination of an **acyl side chain + beta-lactam ring (middle) + thiazolidine ring.** *(Reproduce Figure-2 / Figure-4.)* The beta-lactam ring (penicillin nucleus) is the chief requirement for biological activity, while the side-chain structures determine antibacterial and pharmacological characteristics.

## Q12. What is penicillin? Discuss the history behind its discovery and production.

**Penicillins** are a group of antibiotics derived originally from *Penicillium* moulds. They are **bactericidal** antibacterial drugs that work against a wide range of bacteria by **interfering with bacterial cell-wall synthesis**; they contain the beta-lactam ring. Members include penicillin G, penicillin V, procaine penicillin and benzathine penicillin.

**History:**
- First antibiotic discovered in **1928 by Alexander Fleming** (Professor of Bacteriology, St. Mary's Hospital, London).
- Discovery was **accidental**: returning from holiday on Sept 3, 1928, he found a *Staphylococcus* culture petri dish left open and contaminated; a blob of mould was growing where bacterial growth was inhibited. The mould was *Penicillium notatum*; its "mould juice" killed harmful bacteria (streptococcus, meningococcus, diphtheria bacillus).
- In **1939**, **Howard Florey and Ernst Chain** purified penicillin into powder; in **1941** treated a patient; in **1943** produced it on large scale — helping casualties in **WWII**.
- It was hailed a **miracle drug**; Fleming, Chain and Florey received the **Nobel Prize in Medicine and Physiology in 1945.**

## Q13. Explain the general structure of penicillin and discuss the significance of R and M groups in the side chains.

- Penicillins contain **6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA)** with an **acyl side chain (R)**; structurally = acyl side chain + beta-lactam ring + thiazolidine ring.
- The **beta-lactam ring** is the chief structural requirement for biological activity; the **side-chain (R)** determines antibacterial and pharmacological characteristics.
- **Significance of R (acyl side chain):** the type and nature of penicillin varies with R. Examples (UK/USA designations): 2-Pentenyl (I/F), Benzyl → Penicillin **G** (II), p-Hydroxybenzyl (III/X), n-heptyl (IV/K), Phenoxymethyl → Penicillin **V**. R thus defines which penicillin is formed and its activity (units/mg).
- **Significance of M (the cation/salt in the main ring):** changing M imparts **solubility and ingestion-rate control**:
  - Direct rapid-action injection: M = Na or K
  - Oral tablets: M = K, Ca, Al
  - Delayed-action oil-based injection: M = procaine (imparts limited water solubility)
- *Note:* Penicillin is measured in **units** rather than weight. One unit of penicillin G = activity in 0.6 µg of sodium penicillin; thus 1 mg penicillin sodium = 1667 units.

## Q14. What is an ideal penicillin? Discuss its classification based on mode of production. Describe the side effects.

**Ideal penicillin:** kills pathogenic bacteria causing infections; causes no allergy to host/patient; does not damage host cells; is stable when stored in liquid or solid form.

**Classification based on mode of production:**
1. **Natural penicillins** — produced/harvested naturally by the mould through fermentation; first used clinically; inhibit bacterial cell-wall synthesis. Example: **Penicillin G (benzyl penicillin)** — adding phenoxyacetic acid only enhances yield. (Natural penicillins obtainable directly: penicillin G, V and F.)
2. **Biosynthetic penicillins** — synthesized by the mould **on addition of a precursor**. Example: **Penicillin V (phenoxymethyl penicillin)** — produced only when phenoxyacetic acid is added during fermentation.
3. **Semisynthetic penicillins** — side chain cannot be taken up/activated by mycelia, so it is **chemically attached to 6-APA** (or made enzymatically). They have improved coverage against streptococcal, staphylococcal, aerobic gram-negative and anaerobic organisms; can be given orally or parenterally; combined with aminoglycosides for resistant organisms (e.g., *Pseudomonas*). Examples: **amoxicillin, ampicillin, azidocillin, azlocillin.**

**Side effects:** watery/bloody diarrhoea; fever, chills, body aches, flu symptoms; easy bruising/bleeding, unusual weakness; urinating less or not at all; severe skin rash, itching or peeling; agitation, confusion, unusual thoughts/behaviour; seizure (black-out/convulsions).

## Q16. What is antibiotic resistance and what is the opinion of WHO on it?

**Antibiotic resistance** occurs when **bacteria change in some way that reduces or eliminates the effectiveness of drugs, chemicals or other agents** designed to cure or prevent infections. The bacteria survive and continue to multiply, causing more harm — it is the ability of microbes to resist medication that once successfully treated the patient.

**WHO opinion:**
- Antibiotic resistance is **one of the biggest threats to global health, food security and development** today.
- It can affect anyone, of any age, in any country.
- It occurs naturally, but **misuse of antibiotics in humans and animals accelerates the process**.
- A growing number of infections (pneumonia, tuberculosis, gonorrhoea, salmonellosis) are becoming harder to treat.
- It leads to **longer hospital stays, higher medical costs and increased mortality**.

## Q17. Discuss the large-scale commercial production of penicillin G with the help of a suitable flow chart.

Large-scale production of **Penicillin G (Type-II)** uses the mould *Penicillium chrysogenum* in **aerobic batch fermentation**. *(Reproduce Figure-6 schematic — OUR: oxygen uptake rate, OTR: oxygen transfer rate, MCB: master cell bank, MWCB: manufacturer's working cell bank.)*

Key stages:
- **Inoculum build-up:** culture on agar slant → shake flask (suspension) → seed transfer vessel → seed tank, maintaining desired physicochemical conditions → final fermenter.
- **Medium:** maize-steep liquor base diluted to 4–5% solids, fortified with 4–5% lactose and nutrients (Mg, Zn/Na sulfate, ammonium acetate, potassium dihydrogen sulfate < 0.2%). CaCO₃ added to adjust **pH 5–6**.
- **Precursor:** **phenylacetic acid** added to direct synthesis toward Penicillin G.
- **Sterilization:** moist heat at **115 °C for ½ h**; cool to 20 °C; inoculate with *P. chrysogenum*.
- **Fermentation:** sterile air blown for **4–5 days** at controlled temperature (±½ °C); yield maximized by periodic assay.
- **Recovery:** mould separated by vacuum rotary filter (used as food supplement); penicillin from mycelia-free broth by **solvent extraction** — filtrate adjusted to **pH 2.5** with dilute H₃PO₄, extracted with **amyl acetate**; buffered with sodium salt to pH 7.5; crude penicillin returns to aqueous phase; centrifuged; solvent recirculated.
- **Purification:** second acidic extraction → reversion to pyrogen-free distilled water with alkaline salt (Na, K, Ca, Al) → super-centrifuge → biological filter to remove bacteria/pyrogens → concentrate by **freeze drying or vacuum spray drying.**
- **Crystalline salts** obtained by salting out; further purified by recrystallization. **Procaine penicillin** made by reacting penicillin concentrate (20–30%) with 50% aqueous procaine hydrochloride.

**Significance of precursors:** without a side-chain precursor only natural penicillins form (only benzyl penicillin isolable); adding a suitable precursor gives the desired (semi-synthetic) penicillin (Penicillin G and V are produced commercially), with improved acid stability, β-lactamase resistance and expanded antimicrobial effectiveness.

## Q18. Draw and discuss the schematic diagram for batch production of penicillin.

**Aerobic batch fermentation** *(Reproduce Figure-7 / Figure-9 main fermentation bioreactor):*
- *P. chrysogenum* grown on agar slant is aseptically transferred to a **shake flask** to get a suspension, then to a **seed transfer vessel** for further growth, then to a **seed tank**, maintaining desired physicochemical conditions; when ready, it is transferred to the **final fermentation vessel**.
- **Medium:** maize-steep liquor base (4–5% solids) + 4–5% lactose + nutrients (Mg, Zn/Na sulfate, ammonium acetate, KH₂PO₄ < 0.2%); CaCO₃ to set pH 5–6.
- **Precursor** (phenylacetic acid) added to control type/yield of penicillin (Penicillin G).
- **Sterilization** at 115 °C for ½ h → cool to 20 °C → inoculate.
- **Sterile air** blown for 4–5 days at ±½ °C; broth assayed periodically.
- **Downstream:** vacuum rotary filtration (mould removed) → solvent extraction (amyl acetate at pH 2.5) → buffering/centrifugation → second acidic extraction & purification → super-centrifuge → biological filter → freeze/vacuum-spray drying → crystalline penicillin salts.
