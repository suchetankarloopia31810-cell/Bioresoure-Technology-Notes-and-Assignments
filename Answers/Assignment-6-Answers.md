## Q1. Write a brief note on bioethanol and discuss the process for 2nd generation bioethanol production.

**Bioethanol** is ethyl alcohol (C₂H₅OH) obtained from **bioresources (biomass)** by hydrolysis or sugar fermentation. It is a principal **petrol substitute** for road-transport vehicles. (Ethanol made by reacting ethylene + steam is *not* bioethanol because the source is non-renewable.) It is a clear, colourless, biodegradable, low-toxicity liquid; a **high-octane** fuel that has replaced lead as an octane enhancer; blending with gasoline oxygenates the fuel for cleaner combustion. Common blend: **E10** (10% ethanol, 90% petrol) needs no engine modification; **E85** only for flexible-fuel vehicles.

**2nd generation (2G) bioethanol production (from non-food lignocellulosic feedstock):**
- Feedstocks: dedicated energy crops (switchgrass, miscanthus) and agricultural/wood residues (woodchips, corn stover, sugarcane bagasse, sawdust).
- Cellulosic biomass = cellulose (β(1-4) D-glucose) + hemicellulose (xylose, mannose, galactose, etc.) + **lignin** (a protective barrier hindering depolymerization).
- Process (more complex than 1G):
  1. **Pretreatment** (chemical or enzymatic) to break the polymeric matrix and increase accessibility of C5–C6 sugars.
  2. **Hydrolysis** of cellulose/hemicellulose into fermentable sugars.
  3. **Fermentation** of sugars to ethanol by microbes (direct fermentation via hydrolysis-fermentation, or indirect via **syngas fermentation**).
  4. **Distillation and dehydration** to anhydrous ethanol.
- 2G plants (e.g., POET-DSM, Abengoa — 25 million gallons/year in 2014) are promising; sustainability depends on feedstock availability at reasonable price.

## Q2. Briefly explain the global scenario of biofuel production.

- Bioenergy is increasingly attractive due to rising energy prices, energy security and global-warming concerns.
- **USA and Brazil are world leaders** in bioethanol production (2017); India, despite being a large energy importer, ranks poorly.
- About **104–100 billion litres** of bioethanol produced worldwide (≈2014), with ~80% used as transportation fuel; most consumption is in North & South America (similar amounts in Europe and China).

- Per IRENA, the **advanced biofuels** market (2G/3G/4G feedstocks) is expected to reach **124 billion litres/year by 2030**.

## Q3. Classify and describe bioethanol based on feedstocks.

**First generation (1G):** from **food-based feedstocks** (starch, sugar) — sugarcane, sugar beet, sweet sorghum (sugars) and corn, wheat (starch). Sugars are fermented directly; starch is first hydrolyzed by α-amylase/glucoamylase to glucose, then fermented. Criticized for food competition and land-use change.

**Second generation (2G):** from **non-food lignocellulosic feedstocks** — dedicated energy crops (switchgrass, miscanthus) and agricultural/wood residues. Requires pretreatment to access C5–C6 sugars; lower GHG (CO₂ ≈2.85 kg/kg) but higher capital cost and lower energy density than 1G.

**Third generation (3G):** from **algal biomass** (microalgae/macroalgae). Grows on non-arable land using industrial CO₂ and even wastewater; no fresh-water requirement. Enzymatic hydrolysis is simpler (little/no lignin). Main obstacle: process economics (harvesting = 20–30% of cultivation cost).

**Fourth generation (4G):** bioethanol from **captured CO₂** using advanced technologies (electrochemical synthesis, oxide electrolysis, petroleum hydroprocessing); considered **carbon-negative**, but in an embryonic stage.

## Q4. Write a short note on the important feedstocks suitable for production of bioethanol.

Bioethanol's merit lies in the variety of raw materials. Good feedstock has high carbon/hydrogen and low oxygen/nitrogen. Important feedstocks:
- **Alfalfa** — leaves rich in protein; low glucose yield (low-efficiency cellulose hydrolysis).
- **Switchgrass (*Panicum virgatum*)** — perennial, C4 plant; ethanol yield/ha similar to wheat; adapts to marginal land.
- **Sweet sorghum** — starch-rich grains, high-sucrose stems, lignocellulosic leaves/bagasse; tolerates drought, flooding, alkalinity.
- **Cassava (manioc)** — tropical tuber; must be processed 3–4 days after harvest; wastewater → biogas.
- **Spruce** — lignocellulose rich in easily convertible hexose sugars.
- **Willow (Salix)** — hardwood; acetylated xylose releases acetic acid on pretreatment (yeast inhibitor); ~79% ethanol yield with acid pretreatment + SSF.
- **Reed canary grass** — C3 perennial, flood resistant.
- **Sugarcane** — 12–17% sugar (90% sucrose); milling extracts 95% sugar; bagasse used for energy.
- **Sugar beet** — temperate; pulp used as animal feed.
- **Cereals** (wheat, barley, winter rye, corn stover) — ground for starch; low-protein/high-carbohydrate grains preferred.
- **Jerusalem artichoke** — tubers rich in inulin (fructose polymer).
- **Wastes/residues** — cereal/wheat straw, corn cob, rice husk, bagasse, forest/woody waste, brewer's/distillery spent grains, MSW (food waste, kraft paper, paper sludge), cheese whey, crude glycerol (biodiesel by-product).

## Q5. Discuss the common flow diagram for production of bioethanol from the 1st and 2nd generation feedstocks.

```
   SUGAR crops (1G)        STARCH crops (1G)          CELLULOSIC biomass (2G)
   (cane, beet, sorghum)   (corn, wheat)              (straw, stover, bagasse, wood)
        │                       │                              │
        │                   Milling                      PRETREATMENT
        │                       │                  (chemical/enzymatic; break
        │                LIQUEFACTION (α-amylase)      lignin–cellulose matrix)
        │                       │                              │
        │            SACCHARIFICATION (glucoamylase)     HYDROLYSIS → C5/C6 sugars
        │                       │                              │
        └──────────► FERMENTATION (yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂) ◄───────┘
                                 │
                          DISTILLATION (≈10% v/v beer → conc. ethanol)
                                 │
                          DEHYDRATION → ANHYDROUS ETHANOL
```
- **Sugar feedstocks:** yeast directly consumes simple sugars → ethanol.
- **Starch:** polymer of glucose (amylose α-1,4 linear + amylopectin α-1,4/α-1,6 branched; ~20–30% amylose); must be **gelatinized** (cooking disrupts granules) then hydrolyzed in two stages — **liquefaction** (α-amylase, reduces viscosity) and **saccharification** (glucoamylase → glucose), then fermented.
- **Cellulose (2G):** requires pretreatment + hydrolysis before fermentation (more complex/costly).
- End: beer (~10% v/v ethanol) → distillation + dehydration → anhydrous ethanol.

## Q6. Explain the simplified scheme for bioethanol production from sugarcane.

Sugarcane is the most efficient raw material for bioethanol (lower fossil-energy use than corn). Steps:
1. **Cleaning** — wash impurities (dry cleaning is more eco-friendly, avoids sucrose loss).
2. **Grinding & Juice Extraction** — crush fiber (density 175→450 kg/m³); milling with 4–7 mill suits + hot water (~70 °C) to improve extraction. Remaining **bagasse** (50–52% humidity) → boilers for high-pressure steam → turbine for electrical/mechanical energy.
3. **Clarification** — juice sieved and clarified with sulfate (sulfuric gas) and liming; heated to 105 °C to lower microbial contamination and coagulate colloids; sludge → fertilizer. Residual sugar solution after sugar production = **molasses** (5–20% glucose, 45–60% sucrose).
4. **Fermentation** — sugarcane juice/must (18–22% reducing sugars) + yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*); enzymes sucrase (invertase) and zymase convert sugar:
   - C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + C₆H₁₂O₆
   - C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂
   - Modes: **batch** (lab/small scale), **fed-batch** (~75% of Brazilian mills; higher yield, less contamination), **continuous** (steady-state, but more contamination-prone).
5. **Separation & Distillation** — wine (6–12% v/v alcohol); yeast separated by centrifugation (yeast "cream" recycled after acid treatment); wine distilled → **hydrated ethanol (96° GL)**; by-product **vinasse/stillage** → field fertilizer.
6. **Dehydration** — add cyclohexane (or use molecular sieves) → **anhydrous ethanol**.

## Q7. Make a proper comparison of specifications between gasoline and bioethanol.

| Property | Gasoline | Bioethanol |
|---|---|---|
| Energy content (per volume) | Higher | **~2/3 of gasoline** → reduced vehicle range per tank |
| Octane number | Lower | **Higher** → better antiknock; allows higher compression ratio & efficiency |
| Oxygen content | None | Oxygenated → cleaner, more complete combustion at lower temperature |
| Reid vapour pressure (volatility) | Higher | **Very low** → slow evaporation, low evaporative emissions, less explosion risk |
| Cold start | Easier | Difficult below ~20 °C (single boiling point, low vapour pressure) |
| Water affinity | Immiscible | **Hygroscopic**, miscible with water → corrosion of injector/fuel pump, emulsion risk |
| Use | Standalone | As blend (E5/E10 no modification; E85 only flexible-fuel vehicles); also as **ETBE** (45% bioethanol + 55% isobutylene) octane enhancer instead of carcinogenic MTBE |

## Q8. Discuss India's status in bioethanol production, consumption and requirement.

- India has ~**330 distilleries** producing >4.8 billion L rectified spirit/year; ~166 can distill **2.6 billion L of ethanol**. Feedstocks: C & B-heavy molasses, sugarcane juice, damaged food grains.
- **Consumption (2019):** forecast ~3.8 billion L (up 22%); 5th consecutive year that consumption outgrew production, driven by fuel-ethanol blending demand. Consumption growth ~14%/year vs production ~8%/year.
- **Production (2019):** ~3 billion L (11% rise); a 6.6% blend rate was achievable but national average ~5.8% expected (vs 4.1% in 2018).
- **Requirement:** OMCs set a requirement of ~3.3 billion L for 2019; suppliers offered 3.13 billion L; ~2.7 billion L finalized.
- **Imports:** India remains a **net importer**; the **USA is the largest supplier** (~94% share); 2019 imports up to ~750 million L (highest in a decade); other suppliers: Pakistan, South Africa, UAE, UK.
- **Exports (2019):** expected to fall >20% to ~100 million L (strong domestic demand limits exportable supply).
- **Policy:** Ethanol Blended Petrol (EBP) programme (since 2003); targets **10% blending by 2022 and 20% by 2030**; biodiesel 5% by 2030; **PM JI-VAN Yojana** (2019) funds 2G bioethanol; MoU with Brazil on bioenergy cooperation; plans to raise ethanol capacity to 9 billion L.

## Q9. Write a brief note on bio-aviation turbine fuel and highlight its significance w.r.t. its necessity.

**Bio Aviation Turbine Fuel (Bio-ATF)** is a biofuel for aircraft, prepared from **animal fat, vegetable oil and agricultural waste** — a renewable substitute for petroleum-based jet fuel (kerosene-based, powering gas-turbine engines). Aviation fuel has stricter quality requirements than road fuels.

**Significance / Necessity:**
- Aviation consumes ~1.5–1.7 billion barrels of conventional jet fuel/year (4 of every 42-gallon crude barrel → jet fuel); fuel is the **largest operating cost** and crude price volatility hampers planning.
- Aviation accounts for ~**12% of transport fuel** and ~**2% of GHG emissions**; commercial-flight CO₂ rose 32% from 2013 to 2018.
- Bio-ATF/SAF (from algae, jatropha, waste by-products) can **reduce the carbon footprint by up to 80%** over the lifecycle.
- For aviation, bio-jet fuel is the **only technically viable** low-carbon option (unlike road transport, which can use electricity/solar/hydrogen), so long-term carbon reduction depends on it.
- Targets: carbon-neutral growth and **50% GHG reduction by 2050** (vs 2005); IATA aimed to replace 6% of jet fuel with renewables by 2020.
- India: SpiceJet's first Bio-ATF flight (Dehradun/Chandigarh → Delhi, Aug 2018); IAF expanding biojet-fuel use.

## Q10. Enlist the various production technologies used for bio-ATF and give a diagram explaining production process-wise raw material and technology overview for bio-jet fuel.

**Production technologies (by route):**
- **OTJ (Oil-to-Jet):** from animal/plant tallow (waste vegetable oil, beef tallow, microalgae); includes **HEFA** (Hydrotreated Esters and Fatty Acids) / HRJ, catalytic hydrothermolysis (CH), HDCJ.
- **STJ (Sugar-to-Jet):** catalytic upgrading/conversion of glucose- or starch-based feedstock; includes Direct Sugar to Hydrocarbons (DSHC) + catalytic upgrading.
- **ATJ (Alcohol-to-Jet):** hydrolysis of fiber biomass/glucose → intermediate alcohols (methanol, ethanol, butanol) → dehydration & oligomerization; ethanol-to-jet or butanol-to-jet.
- **GTJ (Gas-to-Jet):** biogas/natural gas/syngas → jet fuel via gas fermentation or **Fischer–Tropsch**.

ASTM D7566 (as of 2016) approves five processes: **FT-SPK, HEFA, SIP (Synthesized Iso-Paraffins), ATJ** (and FT-SPK/A).

**Diagram (raw material → technology → bio-jet fuel):**
```
   Oils/Fats (waste veg oil, tallow, microalgae) ──► OTJ / HEFA (hydrotreating) ──┐
   Sugars / Starch (glucose) ───────────────────────► STJ / DSHC + upgrading ─────┤
   Alcohols (methanol/ethanol/butanol) ─────────────► ATJ (dehydration+oligomer.) ─┼─► BIO-JET FUEL
   Biogas/Syngas (from biomass gasification) ───────► GTJ / Fischer–Tropsch ───────┘     (blended with
                                                                                          conventional ATF)
```

## Q11. How can biojet fuel be manufactured from bio-ethanol? Explain with a simplified flow diagram.

**ATJ (Alcohol-to-Jet) from bio-ethanol:** physicochemical properties of bio-ethanol must be made compatible with petroleum jet fuel. High-purity (**99.5–99.9% anhydrous**) ethanol is used. Steps: remove oxygen by **dehydration**, increase carbon chain by **polymerization (oligomerization)**, then **hydrogenation** to optimize properties.

```
Bio-ethanol (99.5–99.9% anhydrous)
        │  DEHYDRATION (catalyst: alumina / transition-metal oxide / zeolite)
        ▼
   Ethylene (oxygen removed)
        │  OLIGOMERIZATION / POLYMERIZATION (Ziegler–Natta / Cr-diphosphine / zeolite catalyst)
        ▼
   α-Olefins (C4–C20, 96–97% yield)  →  e.g. 5% C4, 50% C6–C10, 30% C12–C14, 12% C16–C18
        │  SELECTIVE DISTILLATION (split light oil & aviation-range hydrocarbons C6–C16)
        │      (low C4–C8 recycled back to oligomerization)
        ▼
   HYDROGENATION (H₂ atmosphere; T, P, flow controlled)  → excess H₂ reused, un-hydrogenated recycled
        ▼
   BIO-ATF  →  blended with conventional ATF
```

## Q12. What is biobutanol? Discuss its significance as a biofuel.

**Biobutanol** is a **4-carbon alcohol (C₄H₉OH, butyl alcohol)** produced by a microbial source from biomass feedstocks (corn grain, lignocellulose, etc.). It is colourless, flammable, miscible with ethanol/ether/organic solvents. Carbon atoms can be straight-chain or branched (isomers). Only **n-butanol** (straight chain) is produced from biomass; all isomers can be made from fossil fuels. Uses: solvent, cosmetics, hydraulic fluids, detergents, drugs, chemical intermediate (butyl acrylate/methacrylate), and as fuel.

**Significance as a biofuel (advantages over ethanol):**
- **Higher energy/heating value and burning efficiency** → longer driving range; air-fuel ratio and energy content **closer to gasoline**.
- Can be **blended with gasoline/diesel in any proportion** and used **without engine retrofit**.
- **Less volatile/explosive**, higher flash point, lower vapour pressure → safer to handle; can be **shipped through existing fuel pipelines**.
- **High hydrophobicity (less hygroscopic)**, less corrosive → avoids ethanol's water absorption, phase separation and acetic-acid corrosion problems.
- Lower evaporative and greenhouse-gas emissions.

## Q13. Tabulate the structures, properties and main applications of N-butanol, 2-butanol, iso-butanol and tert-butanol.

(Isomers differ by position of –OH and carbon-chain structure; properties vary in octane number, boiling point, viscosity, but applications overlap — solvents, industrial cleaners, gasoline additives. Only n-butanol comes from biomass.)

| Isomer | Structure | Key properties | Main applications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **n-Butanol** (1-butanol) | Straight chain CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH (primary) | Higher boiling point/viscosity; **only one producible from biomass** | Biofuel, solvent, plasticizers, butyl acrylate/methacrylate intermediate |
| **2-Butanol** (sec-butanol) | CH₃CH₂CH(OH)CH₃ (secondary) | Moderate volatility | Solvent, chemical intermediate (MEK precursor), industrial cleaner |
| **Iso-butanol** (2-methyl-1-propanol) | (CH₃)₂CHCH₂OH (branched primary) | Higher octane than n-butanol | Solvent, gasoline additive, paints/coatings |
| **Tert-butanol** (2-methyl-2-propanol) | (CH₃)₃COH (tertiary) | High octane; solid near room temp | Octane booster/oxygenate (MTBE/ETBE precursor), solvent, denaturant |

## Q15. Draw a representative schematic diagram for fermentative production of butanol from lignocellulosic biomass.

A typical conversion has **three major steps: Pretreatment → Detoxification → Fermentation** (ABE).

```
LIGNOCELLULOSIC BIOMASS (cellulose + hemicellulose + lignin)
        │
   PRETREATMENT
   • Physical: milling/grinding, extrusion, microwave, ultrasonication
   • Physico-chemical: steam explosion, hydrothermolysis, AFEX, hot water
   • Chemical: alkali, acid, ozonolysis, ionic liquid, organosolv
   • Enzymatic hydrolysis → C6 (glucose) + C5 (xylose) fermentable sugars
        │
   DETOXIFICATION  (remove inhibitors: furfural, HMF, formic/acetic/levulinic acid,
   phenolics) — methods: electrodialysis, (over)liming, activated carbon,
   dilution, resin treatment, filtration/centrifugation
        │
   ABE FERMENTATION  (Clostridium acetobutylicum, anaerobic, biphasic:
   acidogenesis → acids; solventogenesis → Acetone + Butanol + Ethanol)
   modes: batch / fed-batch / continuous (chemostat)
        │
   IN-SITU PRODUCT REMOVAL (gas stripping, pervaporation, adsorption,
   liquid–liquid extraction, pertraction) + DOWNSTREAM SEPARATION (distillation)
        ▼
   BIOBUTANOL (+ acetone, ethanol co-products)
```

## Q16. Discuss the challenges and their solutions with respect to butanol production.

| Challenge | Cause / effect | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **High substrate cost** | Feedstock dominates cost | Use cheap/free lignocellulosic feedstock (wheat straw, corn core, switchgrass); efficient processing |
| **Solvent (butanol) toxicity** | Limits butanol titer to ~2%, high downstream cost | Metabolic engineering/mutagenesis for **butanol-tolerant strains**; **in-situ product removal** (gas stripping, pervaporation, extraction) |
| **Low cell density** | Low productivity | Absorbed-substrate fermentation, **biofilm/immobilized-cell reactors**, cell recycling/bleeding |
| **Substrate inhibition** | High sugar concentration inhibits cells | **Fed-batch** (gradual feeding) keeping substrate below toxic level |
| **By-product formation (acetone, ethanol)** | Low butanol selectivity | Metabolic engineering to disrupt acetone pathway; **homo-butanol** fermentation; convert acetone → isopropanol |
| **Inhibitors from pretreatment** | Furfural, HMF, acids, phenolics, formic acid (acid crash) | **Detoxification** (liming, activated carbon, resins); low-inhibitor pretreatment (organosolv); inhibitor-tolerant strains |
| **Inefficient substrate (xylose) use** | Carbon catabolite repression | Disrupt CCR genes; overexpress xylose transport/catalytic enzymes |
| **High processing cost** | Separate steps | Process integration: **SSF/SSCF**, **consolidated bioprocessing (CBP)** |

## Q17. Write a brief note on the global scenario of bio-butanol market — growth, trends and forecast (2019–2024).

- **Growth:** bio-butanol market expected to register a **CAGR of 8.36%** over 2019–2024.
- **Drivers:** strong carbon-emission reduction and growing role as a **building block for chemical manufacturing**; preferred over bioethanol as a superior automobile fuel (high energy content, less corrosive, blendable in any proportion with gasoline, low vapour pressure → lower volatility/evaporative emissions, fewer GHG emissions).
- **Trends:** USA and European countries began **fleet-testing** bio-butanol; rising vehicle numbers (cars on road projected to reach ~2 billion by 2050; OICA: ~96.8 million vehicles sold in 2017) boost motor-fuel demand.
- **Regional:** **Asia-Pacific dominates**, with **China** holding the largest share (largest paints/coatings and adhesives producer; rapid urbanization, 60% target by 2020).
- **Competitive landscape:** market is in a **nascent, consolidated** stage. **Cathay Industrial Biotech** (Jilin, China) produces corn-based n-butanol and claimed to be the world's largest bio-butanol producer (2017). Potential entrants: Microvi Biotechnologies, Cobalt Technologies, ButaNext.
