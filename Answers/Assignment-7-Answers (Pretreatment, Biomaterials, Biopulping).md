## Q1. Discuss the significance of pretreatment in bioresource technology and explain the basics of hydrolysis in pretreatment.

**Significance of pretreatment:**
- Lignocellulosic biomass (LCB) is abundant, evenly distributed and **carbon neutral** (CO₂ released is balanced by CO₂ absorbed by plants).
- LCB is made of **cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin** bound by covalent and hydrogen bonds → highly **recalcitrant**; lignin resists solubilization and inhibits hydrolysis of cellulose/hemicellulose.
- **Pretreatment breaks these bonds and weakens the association** among cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin → increases digestibility and accessibility of sugars, removes/disrupts lignin, reduces cellulose crystallinity and degree of polymerization.
- It is the **major step** for successful production of biofuels and chemicals; success strongly depends on the pretreatment method. (It contributes ≥ 20% of total conversion cost.)

**Basics of hydrolysis:** A **double decomposition reaction with water as one reactant**: AB + HOH ⇌ AH + BOH. Water breaks a covalent bond by inserting a water molecule across it (opposite of dehydration-condensation). E.g., starch → glucose, fats → glycerol + fatty acids, esters → acid + alcohol (methyl acetate → acetic acid + methanol). In bioresource technology, hydrolysis converts starch, fats/oils and lignocellulosic materials into **simple sugars (glucose/pentose)** which microorganisms then ferment into useful products (e.g., ABE, bioethanol, bio-ATF). Hydrolysis types: physical, chemical, thermochemical, thermophysical, biological (enzyme, acid, alkali hydrolyses are common).

## Q2. Summarize the pretreatment methods by a flow chart diagram and briefly discuss them.

*(Reproduce Figure-6 flow chart of pretreatment processes.)* Pretreatment is broadly classified as:

1. **Physical:** milling, extrusion, microwave, ultrasonication. Reduce particle size → increase surface area, decrease polymerization & crystallinity; eco-friendly, no toxic material; **but high energy consumption.**
2. **Chemical:** alkali, acid, ionic liquids, organosolv, deep eutectic solvents (DES). More effective; alkali solubilizes lignin (saponification), acid solubilizes hemicellulose.
3. **Physicochemical (thermochemical):** steam explosion, ammonia fibre explosion (AFEX), CO₂ explosion, liquid hot water (LHW). Combine mechanical + chemical effects.
4. **Biological:** white-/brown-/soft-rot fungi or enzymes (laccase, lignin peroxidase, manganese peroxidase, versatile peroxidase) degrade lignin; low cost, eco-friendly, no inhibitors; **but slow and long residence time.**

After pretreatment, conversion technologies follow; **primary objectives** are: disrupt the recalcitrant structure, break the lignin sheath, degrade hemicellulose, reduce cellulose crystallinity/polymerization, and increase accessibility for hydrolysis. *(Reproduce Figures-3, 4, 7.)*

## Q3. Discuss the structure and composition of various LCBs.

LCB is mainly composed of three polymers (plus minor proteins, ash, pectin): **cellulose (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ, hemicellulose (C₅H₈O₄)ₘ, and lignin**. Typical ranges: **cellulose 30–60% (or 40–50%), hemicellulose 20–40% (25–35%), lignin 15–25% (15–20%)** — composition varies with source *(Table-1).*

- **Cellulose:** main structural component; **linear polysaccharide of D-glucose linked by β-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds**; insoluble in water (except extreme pH); soluble in ionic liquids and NMMO; crystalline; provides biocompatibility, stereoregularity, hydrophilicity, reactive –OH groups.
- **Hemicellulose:** short chains of different polysaccharides (xylan, galactomannan, glucuronoxylan, arabinoxylan, glucomannan, xyloglucan) linked by β-(1,4)/β-(1,3) bonds; **low polymerization, non-crystalline → readily degradable** to monosaccharides.
- **Lignin:** complex 3-D cross-linked polymer of **phenylpropane units**, linked by aryl-ether (β-O-4, α-O-4) and C–C bonds (5-5, β-β); three basic units — **p-hydroxyphenyl (H), guaiacyl (G), syringyl (S)**; forms a **protective boundary** covalently linking cellulose & hemicellulose → enhances recalcitrance.

Crystallized cellulose + hemicellulose matrix is encrusted by highly polymerized phenolic lignin, causing difficulty in conversion. *(Reproduce Figure-5(a) and 5(b).)*

## Q4. When wood flour is subjected to ultrasonication, how is the disintegration of various components of LCB effected? Sketch the diagram.

**Ultrasonication pretreatment** is based on **cavitation** through ultrasonic radiation. Cavitation generates **shear forces** that cleave the complex network structure of LCB and promote extraction of cellulose, hemicellulose and/or lignin.
- It removes the **amorphous hemicellulose and lignin fractions** → the **crystallinity of cellulose increases** (e.g., eucalyptus wood: 34.7 → 35.3% in soda, 32.6 → 35.5% in water, 33.4 → 35.5% in acetic acid; confirmed by FTIR).
- Choice of solvent (dilute acid/alkali, organic solvents, ionic liquids) is critical; influencing factors: **ultrasound frequency, sonication duration, power, temperature**.
- Ultrasound can reduce hydrolysis time by up to **80%**. (Prolonged sonication/high power causes particle collision/aggregation and cavitation near the transducer tip, limiting energy transfer.)

**Diagram — disintegration of wood flour:** *(Reproduce Figure-13.)* **Cellulose nanocrystals (CNCs)** were extracted from wood flour by a **two-step process**: (1) **ethanol + peroxide solvothermal pretreatment** (removes lignin/hemicellulose) followed by (2) **ultrasonic disintegration** (cavitation shear breaks the fibres into nanocellulose). The diagram shows wood flour → solvothermal pretreatment → ultrasonic disintegration → CNCs.

## Q5. Write short notes on chemical, physical, physicochemical and biological methods of pretreatment.

**Physical:** Mechanical methods — **milling** (ball, rod, hammer, vibratory, colloid, wet-disk; reduces particle size to ~0.2 mm and lowers crystallinity; no toxic compounds; high energy demand), **extrusion** (one/two screws in a heated barrel; shear + high temperature disrupts structure), **microwave** (dielectric heating; energy efficient, minimal inhibitors), **ultrasonication** (cavitation shear). Increase surface area; decrease crystallinity & polymerization.

**Chemical:** **Alkali** (NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₄OH; saponification cleaves ester bonds between lignin & hemicellulose, solubilizes lignin, swells cellulose; best for low-lignin biomass; NaOH most effective). **Acid** (dilute or concentrated H₂SO₄, HCl, CH₃COOH; primarily **solubilizes hemicellulose** (xylan), disrupts lignin; dilute acid preferred — concentrated acid gives more inhibitors HMF/furfural and corrosion). **Ionic liquids** (green solvents, dissolve cellulose/lignin; recyclable; but expensive/toxic to enzymes). **Organosolv** (organic solvents break lignin–hemicellulose bonds, leave pure cellulose; solvents recoverable; flammable). **Deep eutectic solvents (DES)** (low cost, biodegradable; ChCl-based).

**Physicochemical (thermochemical):** **Steam explosion** (high-pressure saturated steam 0.69–4.83 MPa, 160–260 °C then sudden pressure release → fibres split; autohydrolysis). **AFEX** (liquid ammonia ~60–100 °C under pressure then release; swells lignocellulose, low inhibitors; best for low-lignin biomass). **CO₂ explosion** (supercritical CO₂; greener, lower energy; forms carbonic acid). **Liquid hot water (LHW)** (water at 170–230 °C, ≤5 MPa; hydrolyzes hemicellulose; pH 4–7; energy intensive).

**Biological:** lignin-degrading bacteria/fungi or enzymes (laccase, LiP, MnP, versatile peroxidase) pretreat LCB; **white-rot fungi** most effective (*Phanerochaete chrysosporium* model organism). Low cost, eco-friendly, no inhibitors, low energy; **drawbacks:** low efficiency and long residence times.

## Q6. How is the position of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin affected in enzyme pretreatment?

In **enzymatic (biological) pretreatment**, microorganisms (fungi/bacteria) secrete two extracellular enzyme systems that act on different components:
- **Hydrolytic system** → degrades **cellulose and hemicellulose** (cellulolytic and hemicellulolytic enzymes).
- **Ligninolytic system** → depolymerizes **lignin** (phenol oxidase/laccase, and peroxidases — lignin peroxidase LiP, versatile peroxidase VP, manganese peroxidase MnP; heme-containing glycoproteins needing H₂O₂).

Effect on the three components *(Figure-20):*
- **Lignin** is **selectively degraded/removed** first (it is the protective barrier). White-rot fungi attack the aromatic lignin polymer and aliphatic fragments, breaking the lignin sheath; some white-rot fungi selectively degrade lignin while leaving carbohydrates relatively intact.
- **Hemicellulose** is degraded by hemicellulolytic enzymes (xylanases), exposing the inner structure.
- **Cellulose** becomes **more exposed/accessible** for subsequent enzymatic saccharification (some non-selective fungi also degrade cellulose → carbohydrate loss).

Thus enzyme pretreatment **removes/loosens lignin and hemicellulose**, repositioning/exposing the cellulose fibres so that hydrolytic enzymes can access them → higher sugar yield. **Laccases** oxidize phenolic (and, with mediators, non-phenolic) lignin substructures; **LiP/MnP** depolymerize lignin.

## Q7. Explain biomaterials and cite at least ten examples.

**Biomaterial** is a **synthetic (or natural) material used to make devices that replace a part of a living system or function in intimate contact with living tissue**. They are of metallic, ceramic, polymeric or composite origin, designed to **augment, replace or restore the function of diseased/damaged tissues or organs**, and engineered to interact favourably with biological organisms or molecules. Used in tissue engineering, biosensing and drug delivery. They may be derived from organisms or be synthesized polymers; modern designs incorporate biologically-derived/inspired materials (silk, collagen).

**Ten examples:** stainless steel (316L) bone pins/plates; titanium and Ti-alloy (Ti-6Al-4V) joint/hip implants; cobalt-base alloys; alumina and zirconia (ceramic implants); hydroxyapatite/calcium phosphate (bone grafts); PMMA (intraocular lenses, bone cement); polyethylene (UHMWPE) joint components; silicone/polysiloxane (soft-tissue, breast implants); polyurethane (cardiac/vascular devices); poly-glycolic acid (resorbable sutures); contact lenses; heart valves; carbon (and PLA-coated carbon-fibre composites); blood bags/syringes.

## Q8. Write a short note on the history of biomaterials.

- The field of biomaterials is relatively new compared with materials science. Ancient cases exist (glass eyes, metallic/wooden dental implants in ancient Egypt).
- The modern age depended on **aseptic surgical techniques pioneered by Sir Joseph Lister** (mid-19th century) and on the **development of synthetic polymers** just before, during and after **World War II**.
- **Before WWII:** implanted biomaterials were mainly **metals** (steel pins, plates, joint replacements, bone-defect covers).
- **Late 1940s:** **Harold Ridley** observed that PMMA shards from aircraft windshields lodged in WWII aviators' eyes provoked little inflammatory response → led to **PMMA intraocular lenses** and greater use of polymers in contact with tissue.
- **1970s–80s:** growth of cellular, molecular and developmental biology gave new insights → a **paradigm shift**: from inert, non-degradable replacements to materials that **interact with biology** (incorporating bioactive molecules, therapeutics, motifs). This led to a polymer/composite revolution and a dramatic increase in biomaterials publications (47,622 publications during 1999–2013).

**Evolving definitions:** 1974 **Clemson Advisory Board / WHO** — "a systemically pharmacologically inert substance designed for implantation within or incorporation with living tissue"; **Dr. Jonathan Black** broadened it; **NIH (1983)** — "any substance (other than a drug) or combination, synthetic or natural, used for any period as whole/part of a system that treats, augments or replaces any tissue, organ or function of the body."

## Q9. Discuss the applications of biomaterials.

Biomaterials are introduced into the body as medical devices. **Medical applications:** cancer therapy; artificial ligaments and tendons; **orthopaedics** (joint replacements, bone plates); **ophthalmic** (contact lenses, cornea/eye-lens replacement); **wound healing** (surgical sutures, clips); nerve regeneration; **reproductive therapy** (breast implants); cardiovascular implants (heart valves, cardiac assist devices, artificial blood vessels, pacemakers); artificial organs (heart, kidney, liver, pancreas, bladder); dental implants; artificial skin; drug-delivery and tissue engineering.

**Non-medical applications:** growing cells in culture medium; assay of blood proteins in laboratories; non-corrective custom contact lenses.

High-volume products include **blood bags, syringes and needles**, ranging up to challenging implantable devices to replace diseased organs. *(Reproduce Tables 1–4 and Figures 4–7 — orthopaedic, dentistry, ophthalmic applications.)*

## Q10. Define biocompatibility and discuss the requirement for implantation.

**Biocompatibility** is related to the **behaviour of biomaterials in various environments under various chemical and physical conditions**. In a simple sense, materials are biocompatible when they **exert the expected beneficial tissue response and clinically relevant performance** (with the organism accepting the foreign material as part of the whole, without harmful effects at the operation site). Other components of biocompatibility: **cytotoxicity, genotoxicity, mutagenicity, carcinogenicity and immunogenicity.** It comprehends toxicity, tissue compatibility, blood compatibility (**hemocompatibility**) and biofunctionality.

**Requirements for implantation / to be a biomaterial:** besides not inducing inflammation, toxic reactions or allergenic symptoms, it should be **biocompatible, biofunctional, bioactive, bioinert and sterilizable**; hygienization/sterilization must not modify its properties. The surface (directly exposed to the living organism) is crucial. The idea of a *totally inert* material has been abandoned — any material elicits some body response varying with application and patient (age, sex). Based on body response, biomaterials are characterized as:
- **Biotolerable** — tolerated, separated by fibrous tissue (most synthetic polymers, most metals);
- **Bioinert** — tolerated, no chemical reaction (alumina, zirconia, titanium, carbon);
- **Bioactive** — make chemical bonds with bone (osseointegration) (calcium phosphate, hydroxyapatite, bioglass/vitroceramics);
- **Biodegradable** — degrade/solubilize/absorb in the body (biopolymers; no second surgery needed).

## Q11. Describe the characteristics and properties of biomaterials.

**Characteristics required:** biocompatible, biofunctional, bioactive, bioinert, sterilizable; should not induce inflammation, toxicity or allergy. **Biofunctionality** is essential — the material must perform its designed function for the required time; for long-lasting use it must not degrade and must retain chemical/mechanical stability. *(Reproduce Figure-9.)*

**Properties:**
- **Physical properties:** fundamental for cell-adhesion response; surface properties include **wettability, filler, roughness, softness, chemical composition** (influence physical-chemical reactions between cell and biomaterial).
- **Chemical properties:** determine chemical stability and reactivity; the corporeal ambience is harsh and may cause **corrosion** → adverse reactions, ion-concentration increase (swelling/pain), migration of corrosion products, loss of mechanical integrity. **Surface functional groups** (–COOH, –OH, –NH₂, –CH₃) affect adsorbed protein and cell/protein interactions. Degradation alters structure/properties.
- **Mechanical properties:** **Young's modulus, ductility, tensile strength, yield strength, compression strength, fatigue, wear debris** — chosen to match the tissue (elasticity ranges from ~0.1–1 kPa for soft brain tissue to ≥30 kPa for mineralized bone). Materials with Young's modulus close to bone are recommended (uniform stress distribution, avoid **stress shielding**); high yield/compression strength avoids fractures; ductility helps modelling/dental applications.

## Q12. How is the selection of biomaterials made? Discuss.

The selection should follow a **logical sequence**:
1. **Analysis of the problem**;
2. **Consideration of requirements**;
3. **Consideration of available materials and their properties** — leading to →
4. **Choice of material.**

The choice of a specific biomedical material is determined by:
1. A **proper specification of the desired function** for the material;
2. An **accurate characterization of the environment** in which it must function, and the effects that environment will have on the material's properties;
3. A **delineation of the length of time** the material must function;
4. A **clear understanding of what is meant by "safe for human use."**

The choice is associated with the **function it will perform and the time it will contact the body**, based on a combination of physical, chemical, mechanical and biological properties.

## Q13. Discuss the most common classification of biomaterials.

The most common classes are **polymers, metals and ceramics** (used singly or in combination, plus composites):

- **Polymers:** acrylics, polyamides, polyesters, polyethylene, polysiloxanes, polyurethane, reprocessed biological materials. Uses: EVA membranes (controlled release), poly-glycolic acid (resorbable sutures), artificial organs, bone cement, catheters, contact lenses, heart valves, cardiac assist devices, soft-tissue replacement, artificial blood vessels/skin, sutures.
- **Metals:** (a) iron-base **316L stainless steel**; (b) **titanium & Ti-alloys** (Ti-6Al-4V, Ti-Ni 55/45 shape-memory); (c) **cobalt-base alloys** (Cr-Mo-Ni, etc.). Most common implant metals: 316L stainless steel, Ti-6Al-4V, cobalt-base alloys; also niobium and shape-memory alloys.
- **Composites:** e.g., UHMWPE matrix reinforced with **carbon fibres** (6–15 µm, randomly oriented); stronger than single materials; need good fibre-matrix interfacial bond. Examples: Al₂O₃ on carbon, carbon/PTFE, Al₂O₃/PTFE, PLA-coated carbon fibres.
- **Ceramics:** aluminium oxides, calcium phosphates, apatites, graphite, glasses. Chosen for **inertness, formability into shapes/porosities, high compressive strength, excellent wear**. Applications: hip prostheses, artificial knees, bone grafts, tissue-ingrowth in orthopaedics, dentistry, heart valves.

## Q14. What is biopulping? Discuss its significance.

**Biopulping** is the **fungal pretreatment of wood chips** for production of mechanical or chemical pulps. Its concept is based on the ability of a restricted number of **white-rot fungi** to **colonize and selectively degrade lignin in wood, leaving cellulose relatively intact**. It is an **environmentally friendly technology** that increases mill throughput or reduces electrical energy consumption.

**Significance:**
- **Energy saving:** produces stronger pulp with longer fibres and increased fibrillation; reduces electrical energy in mechanical-pulp refining (the major cost). MnP treatment cut specific energy ~11% (Scots pine), ~6% (Norway spruce); *Phanerochaete chrysosporium* saves ~30% of fiberizing/refining energy.
- **Reduced chemicals (chemical pulping):** improved cooking → reduced **kappa numbers/active alkali charge and cooking time** after 1–2 weeks of fungal treatment.
- **Improved pulp quality:** better brightness, strength, bleachability; reduced pitch content; biopulps easier to refine.
- **Environmental:** reduces pulping chemicals, **effluent pollution load (BOD, COD, AOX, colour)** and hazardous gas emissions; steaming chips before treatment also eradicates sporulating fungi that cause respiratory problems in mill workers.
- It coincides with environmentally safe production and can be implemented in existing plants without major changes. *(Reproduce Table-3: benefits.)*

## Q15. Describe the concerns of conventional pulping process.

The pulp and paper industry has major impacts on natural ecosystems, air/water quality and human health. Concerns fall under **Resources, Processing and Disposal**:

- **Impact on forests:** demand for timber/fibre → logging of natural (even pristine boreal) forests and replacement by monoculture plantations with fewer ecological/social benefits.
- **Excess use of water:** the industry is a large water user; efforts focus on reducing usage, recirculation/reuse and recycling cleaned water.
- **Pollutants in water:** suspended solids (fibre), **BOD/COD**, toxicity and colour. Suspended solids removed by clarifier settling (80–90%); BOD reduced by good washing, black-liquor recovery, oxygen delignification and biological treatment (stabilization/aerated basins, activated sludge). Bleaching effluent contains toxic/genotoxic/mutagenic substances, especially **chlorinated organics** (hence shift from chlorine to chlorine dioxide). Colour mainly from bleaching.
- **Atmospheric emissions:** SO₂, NOx, chlorine dioxide, and **reduced sulphur gases** (H₂S, methyl mercaptan, dimethyl sulphide/disulphide — the typical "kraft mill odour"); particulate "saltcake" (Na₂SO₄), lime dust, fly ash — controlled by scrubbers and electrostatic precipitators.
- **Disposal:** consequences of waste-paper disposal.
- Lignin and degradation products are the most important contributors to environmental pollution; manufacturing uses lots of chemicals and electrical energy.

## Q16. Give an overview of the steps involved in the biopulping process with the help of a simple diagram.

*(Reproduce Figure-12: overview of biopulping.)* In biopulping, wood chips are pretreated with fungal inoculum before refining; treated chips become **softened and more porous**, breaking apart more easily during refining → substantial energy saving. Steps:
1. **Decontamination:** wood chips treated/steamed briefly (as short as 15 s) to eliminate competitive natural bacteria and fungi.
2. **Cooling & Inoculation:** chips sprayed with a **dilute inoculum of a selected (white-rot) fungus**.
3. **Incubation:** inoculated chips incubated in an **aerated chip pile for ~2 weeks** under warm, moist conditions; lignin-degrading fungi colonize chip surfaces and penetrate interiors with a network of **hyphae**.
4. **Refining/Pulping:** treated chips are more readily broken apart → flexible, intact fibres → mechanical or chemical pulping.

*Ceriporiopsis subvermispora* is very competitive on both softwoods and hardwoods (produces MnP and laccase isoenzymes). Ligninases attack lignin; xylanases degrade hemicellulose making pulp more permeable for residual lignin removal; biopulping also reduces pitch and effluent toxicity/BOD. Existing pulping plants need no additional equipment (only asepsis/inoculation systems — conveyor, steam, inoculum delivery).

## Q17. List the white-rot fungi species used for biopulping.

*Coriolus versicolor, Dichomitus squalens, Phellinus pini, Phlebia tremellosus, Poria medullapanis, Scytinostroma galactinum, Coriolopsis rigida, Coriolus versicolor* var. *antarcticus, Peniophora* sp., *Phanerochaete sordida, Pycnoporus sanguineus, Steccherinum* sp., *Trametes elegans* and *Trametes villosa.*

Other prominent species mentioned: **Ceriporiopsis subvermispora** (very competitive on soft & hardwoods) and **Phanerochaete chrysosporium** (model organism for lignin degradation; ~30% energy saving).

**Criteria for a fungus used commercially in biopulping:** relatively fast growth rate; ability to grow on both hardwood and softwood; preferred action against hemicellulose and lignin with low activity on cellulose; should not elicit allergies in workers; small pigmentation (so as not to decrease pulp brightness); good capability to sporulate (for inoculation of chips).

## Q18. Write a brief note on the constraints of biopulping.

- **No single organism is ideal** for all biopulping applications.
- The chosen organism must accomplish **delignification in a reasonable period** (biological methods are **slow in reaction rate** with long residence times).
- **High moisture content (≈55–60%)** must be maintained in wood chips for optimal fungal colonization and hyphal penetration.
- The **degree of asepsis must be controlled** to ensure successful colonization by the particular fungal strain (its resistance to contamination and ability to compete with the existing wood microbiota).
- There is **no clear correlation** between biopulping efficiency and wood weight or component losses.
- **Enzymes** are precise but their action on wood is restricted due to relatively high molecular weight (though more easily applicable to mill scale).
- **Complexity in process control** is a major limitation.
- Removal of minor wood components (resins, polyphenols/extractives) may relate to biopulping gain.
