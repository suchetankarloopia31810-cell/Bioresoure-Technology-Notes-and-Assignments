## Q1. Write a brief note on activated carbon.

**Activated carbon** (also called **activated charcoal**, activated coke or active carbon) is a form of carbon processed to have small, low-volume **pores** that greatly increase the **surface area** available for adsorption or chemical reactions.
- It has a high degree of **microporosity**; surface area can range from ~1500 to 3000 m²/g (gas adsorption).
- Surface area varies with the precursor (raw material) and carbonization conditions.
- Composed of a microporous, homogeneous structure with high surface area and radiation stability; adsorption capacity depends on **porosity and surface chemistry**.
- Usually derived from charcoal (from coal → activated coal; from coke → activated coke).
- Prepared by **chemical or physical activation** and microwave radiation methods.
- Used by separation and purification industries as an **adsorbent**.
- Available in different forms: **powder, granules and pellets**.

## Q2. Why is there a need for switching over from traditional feedstock (fossil origin) to bioresources for production of activated carbon?

- Traditional AC was made from **coal, lignite, peat, petroleum residue and wood**, which are **expensive, exhaustible and non-renewable**.
- Interest has grown in AC made from **agricultural, municipal, industrial and forestry bio-wastes** because these are **cheap, easily available, renewable and ecologically suitable**.
- Carbon made from biomass shows **excellent surface properties, high porosity and high specific surface area**.
- Examples of agricultural waste: palm shells, fruit shells, groundnut shell, coconut shell, *Ricinus communis* seed shell; also root, stem, bark, flower, leaf, fruit peel, husk, shell, stone.
- Fossil feedstocks cannot be a permanent choice due to **environmental concerns, high cost and non-renewability**, whereas bioresources offer low cost, easy availability and environmental compatibility.
- AC from bioresources costs much less than commercial AC and contributes to **environmental sustainability**.

## Q3. Discuss in short the various biomass feedstocks for activated carbon.

Biomass covers all organic material (living plants, plant waste, animal waste). It is divided into **waste materials** and **dedicated energy crops**. The main feedstocks are:
- **Dedicated energy crops** — grown on less fertile/arid land; two types: *herbaceous* (bamboo, wheatgrass, sorghum) and *woody* (fast-growing hardwoods: poplar, willow, maple, walnut).
- **Forest residues** — obtained after logging timber or from excess biomass in forests; reduces fire/pest risk and improves forest vitality.
- **Agricultural crop residues** — wheat, barley, rye, oat, maize, rice, rapeseed, sunflower; abundant and widely distributed, providing complementary rural income.
- **Algae** — microalgae, seaweed, cyanobacteria; grow in fresh, saline, brackish or wastewater; very promising feedstock.
- **Municipal Solid Waste (MSW)** — household wastes (food waste, paper, wood trimmings, textiles); use reduces landfill volume.
- **Wet waste** — food waste, sewage sludge, manure slurries, industrial organic wastes and biogas from anaerobic decomposition.

Biomass is classified as **woody** (cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin) and **non-woody** (also containing lipids, protein, sugar, water, hydrocarbons, starch, functional groups).

## Q4. What are the different types of activated carbon? How is the structure explained?

**Three main types:**
1. **Powdered Activated Carbon (PAC)** — particle size ~5–150 Å (very fine); used in liquid-phase adsorption; reduced processing cost and operational flexibility.
2. **Granular Activated Carbon (GAC)** — 0.2–5 mm; used in gas and liquid phases; clean handling, longer life, higher hardness/strength, can be regenerated and reused.
3. **Extruded Activated Carbon (EAC)** — cylindrical pellets 1–5 mm; heavy-duty, used mainly in gas-phase reactions.

**Additional types:** Bead Activated Carbon (BAC, spherical, petroleum-pitch based), Impregnated Carbon (chemisorption/bacteriostatic), Polymer-Coated Carbon (for hemoperfusion), Activated Carbon Cloths (ACC, originally for chemical-warfare suits), and Activated Carbon Fibers (ACF, fast adsorption).

**Structure:**
- X-ray diffraction shows a structure similar to **graphite** — layers of fused hexagons ~0.34 nm apart held by Van der Waals forces (ABAB lattice in ideal graphite).
- Activated carbon is composed of tiny **graphite-like platelets**, a few atoms thick and 20–100 Å in diameter, forming the **walls of pores** (cavities of molecular dimensions).
- The hexagonal rings are randomly oriented (many cleaved), giving a **disordered "turbostratic" structure** with larger inter-layer spacing (~3.60 Å) than graphite.
- Structural imperfections allow **oxygen-containing surface functional groups** (carboxylic acid, phenolic hydroxyl, quinone carbonyl, lactones, anhydrides, cyclic peroxides) at the edges of broken graphitic rings.

## Q5. Briefly summarize the properties of activated carbon. How are the pores so important (explain with a diagram)?

**Properties** (physical: particle density, bulk density, pore volume, ball-pan hardness, particle size distribution, ash, moisture, carbon content; chemical/adsorption: surface area, iodine number, CTC number, benzene number):
- **Pore structure** — crude amorphous graphite, highly porous; very high internal surface area (up to ~1500 m²/g); pore volume >0.2 ml/g, surface area >400 m²/g (BET).
- **Hardness/abrasion** — coconut-shell AC has the highest hardness.
- **Adsorptive properties** — adsorption driven by **London dispersion (Van der Waals) forces**; chemisorption involves stronger chemical bonds. London forces are additive, non-specific, temperature-independent and short-ranged.
- **CTC activity** — measure of pore volume (now replaced by Butane activity).
- **Apparent/bulk density**, **moisture** (ideally 3–6%), **ash content** (lower is better; coconut 2–3%, wood 5%, coal 8–15%), **pH** (coconut AC pH 9–11), **particle size**, **iodine number** (indicates available surface area), and **surface area** (BET, m²/g).

**Importance of pores (IUPAC classification):**
- **Macropores** (>50 nm) — "highways" into the particle.
- **Mesopores** (2–50 nm) — transport channels; crucial for kinetics.
- **Micropores** (<2 nm) — contribute the **major part of the internal surface area** where adsorption occurs.

```
   Adsorbate ──► Macropore (>50 nm) ──► Mesopore (2–50 nm) ──► Micropore (<2 nm)
                 [transport "highways"]                       [most surface area / adsorption]
```
The combination of pore structure and attractive forces is what allows adsorption to take place, so pore size distribution directly controls capacity and rate.

## Q6. Describe the process for production of activated carbon with the help of a suitable flow diagram.

AC is produced by two main processes: **Carbonization** and **Activation**.

**Carbonization** — "formation of carbon": carbonaceous biomass is heated in the **absence of air** (inert atmosphere, below ~800 °C) → thermal/pyrolytic decomposition (dehydrogenation, condensation, hydrogen transfer, isomerization) → removes O, H, N, S → leaves **biochar** (solid porous carbon).

**Activation** — the char is activated to develop the pore structure:
- **Physical/thermal activation** — oxidizing char at 800–900 °C with air, CO₂ or steam (two-stage process).
- **Chemical activation** — single-stage, char treated with chemicals (KOH, NaOH, H₃PO₄, ZnCl₂).

**General flow diagram:**
```
Bioresource feedstock (coconut/fruit stones/wood/husk, etc.)
   ↓ cleaning, sizing, upstream processing
Impregnation with reagent-1
   ↓
CARBONIZATION (inert, <800 °C) → charcoal/biochar (with porosity)
   ↓ wash, dry
Impregnation with reagent-2
   ↓
ACTIVATION (physical/chemical, specified conditions)
   ↓ wash, dry
ACTIVATED CARBON → desired application
```
Two main industrial methods: **wet-chemical process** (single-stage, uses chemicals) and **physical/thermal-gas process** (two-stage, uses CO₂/air/steam).

## Q7. Depict various possible routes for synthesis of active carbon from biomass with the help of a diagram.

Carbon-based adsorbents can be made from any high-carbon precursor; biomass is ideal. Routes:
```
                         BIOMASS (agricultural/industrial waste)
                                     │
        ┌────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────┐
   Carbonization                Physical activation            Chemical activation
        │                       (CO₂/steam/air, 800–900 °C)     (KOH/NaOH/H₃PO₄/ZnCl₂)
   BIOCHAR                            │                                │
   (less porosity)        ──►  ACTIVATED CARBON (developed porosity) ◄──
                                     │
              Non-conventional routes: Microwave heating, Hydrothermal carbonization
                          (for energy / chemical savings)
```
- **Biochar** is obtained upon carbonization.
- **Activated carbon** is obtained by physical or chemical activation (more developed porosity).
- Self-activation (using pyrolysis gases) and non-conventional methods (microwave, hydrothermal) save chemicals/energy.
- Porosity, surface chemistry and yield depend on starting biomass composition and operating conditions.

## Q8. What are the different applications of activated carbon? Discuss the factors affecting production.

**Applications:** Activated carbon is highly versatile due to its superior adsorbent capability and high surface area. Industries: petroleum, fertilizer, nuclear, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, textiles, automobile, vacuum manufacturing. Specific uses:
- **Metals recovery** (gold, silver).
- **Food & beverage** (decaffeination; removal of odour/taste/colour).
- **Medicinal** (treating poisonings/ailments).
- **Air emission purification** (VOCs, odours).
- **Biogas purification** (removing H₂S, siloxanes, terpenes; >99% CH₄).
- **Remediation** of contaminated soil/groundwater.
- **Chemicals purification** (product purity).
- **Wastewater treatment** (COD, BOD, TOC, BTEX, hydrocarbons, AOX, pesticides, cyanides, odour, colour).
- Also solvent recovery, gas separation, dye removal, and as a **catalyst in biodiesel production**.
- AC can be **regenerated/reactivated** (commonly thermal reactivation in a rotary kiln; also chemical, microbial, electrochemical, ultrasonic, wet-air oxidation).

**Factors (parameters) affecting AC production:**
1. **Raw materials** — need high carbon, low inorganic/ash, high density and volatile content, low storage degradation, cheap, stable supply (lignocellulosics ≈45% of feedstock; coconut shell & fruit stones most common for hard GAC).
2. **Temperature** — activation temperature (200–1100 °C; commercially >800 °C) strongly affects surface area and yield; higher temperature → higher BET surface area but **lower AC yield** (more volatiles released, more fixed carbon/ash).
3. **Activation time** — typically 1–3 h; longer time → higher BET surface area but **lower yield**.
4. **Type and concentration of chemicals** used in impregnation/chemical activation also affect yield and characteristics.

## Q9. Write a brief note on biofuels. Discuss the relevance of biofuel technology for developing countries like India.

**Biofuel** is a fuel (gas, liquid or solid) derived from **biomass** (plant/algae material or animal waste). Since feedstock can be replenished, biofuel is a **renewable** energy source (unlike fossil fuels). Crops used are high in sugar (sugarcane, sugar beet, sweet sorghum), starch (maize, tapioca) or oils (soybean, rapeseed, coconut, sunflower). Liquid biofuels include ethanol, methanol, biodiesel and Fischer–Tropsch diesel; gaseous biofuels include hydrogen and methane. A key chemical difference from petroleum is the **high oxygen content (10–45%)** and low sulfur.

**Relevance for developing countries like India:**
- **Energy security** and reduced dependence on imported oil (foreign-exchange savings).
- **Environmental concerns** — biofuels are biodegradable, environmentally friendly, and combustion follows the CO₂ cycle (carbon-neutral character).
- **Socioeconomic/rural development** — biomass is widely available, so biofuel technology can employ more people than fossil-fuel technology, supporting rural economies.
- Helps transition from a **hydrocarbon economy to a carbohydrate economy** (bioethanol/biomethanol replacing oil-based fuels), supporting GDP growth, lower oil imports, lower CO₂ emissions and direct job creation.

## Q10. Discuss the significance of Fischer–Tropsch (FT) synthesis technology for biofuel production.

- The **Fischer–Tropsch process** is a collection of chemical reactions that convert a mixture of **CO + H₂ (syngas)** into **liquid hydrocarbons** over metal catalysts (Ru, Co, Fe), typically at **150–300 °C** and pressures of one to several tens of atmospheres.
- First developed by **Franz Fischer and Hans Tropsch in Germany, 1925**.
- **Significance for biofuels:** syngas from **biomass gasification** (including lignocellulosic residues, a low-cost feedstock) becomes the feedstock for FT conversion to **bio-syngas–derived synthetic fuels**, replacing non-renewable fossil fuels. The integrated process (biomass gasification + FTS) turns a renewable feedstock into a **clean fuel**.
- Products are mainly **aliphatic straight-chain hydrocarbons (CₓHy)** plus minor branched/unsaturated hydrocarbons and primary alcohols: CH₄, ethene/ethane, LPG (C3–C4), gasoline (C5–C12), diesel (C13–C22), waxes (C23–C33).
- Product distribution depends on the **catalyst and process parameters** (temperature, pressure, residence time). Catalyst factors (active-phase state, promoters, size, microenvironment) determine CO conversion and selectivity (especially to C5+ hydrocarbons).
- FT is crucial for converting non-petroleum carbon resources (coal, natural gas, shale gas, coal-bed gas, biogas, biomass) into liquid fuels and chemicals.

## Q11. Briefly discuss the global scenario with respect to biofuel production.

- Bioenergy has gained attention as a **sustainable energy source** that copes with rising energy prices and provides income to poor farmers/rural communities.
- Drivers: rising fuel prices, growing energy demand, global-warming concerns, openness to renewables, domestic energy security, and new crop markets.
- Despite interest, there are still **few players** in the field.
- **Bioethanol (2017):** the **USA and Brazil are world leaders**; India, though one of the largest energy importers, has a very poor ranking.
- **Biodiesel:** production/consumption increasing globally as the best short-term substitute for mineral diesel; controversies include feedstock availability/cost, GHG emissions, land-use change, and food-vs-fuel competition (overcome using non-crop feedstocks).
- Since 2000, global biofuel supply rose many-fold by 2010 (driven by blending mandates). For 2013–2015 the global supply was roughly a **3:1 ratio of ethanol to biodiesel**, mostly conventional biofuels from sugar, starch, vegetable oil or animal fat.

## Q13. How are biofuels classified on the basis of feedstock? Discuss them.

**Broad classification:** Primary and Secondary biofuels.
- **Primary biofuels** — natural, unprocessed biomass (firewood, wood chips, pellets); directly combusted for cooking/heating/electricity.
- **Secondary biofuels** — produced by processing biomass into solids (charcoal), liquids (ethanol, biodiesel, bio-oil) or gases (DME); used in vehicles and industry.

**Secondary biofuels are sub-classified by generation (feedstock):**
1. **First generation** — from **food crops** (sugar, starch, vegetable oil, animal fat): soybean, palm, canola, rapeseed → bioethanol/biodiesel; problem: competes with food, land and water use.
2. **Second generation (advanced)** — from **non-food crops/waste biomass** (cellulosic material, wheat/corn stalks, wood, waste cooking oil); less food competition, lower GHG. (Virgin vegetable oil = 1st gen, but used/waste oil = 2nd gen.)
3. **Third generation** — from **algae** ("oilgae"); low-cost, high-yielding (up to ~30× energy per unit area vs first-generation feedstocks).
4. **Fourth generation** — from **engineered/metabolically modified algae** (oxygenic photosynthetic microorganisms) to maximize biofuel output and reduce GHG.

*(FAO classification also groups biofuels as wood fuels, agro-fuels and municipal by-products.)*

## Q14. What are the various key issues and performance considerations with respect to biofuel sustainability?

Key sustainability issues (ecological, social, economic):
- **Food–Fuel debate** — competition for cropland; biofuel growth linked (≈20–40% by some estimates) to the 2006–2008 / 2010–2011 food-price surges (along with oil prices, weather, speculation).
- **Emissions** — mixed claims; biofuels may reduce GHG by 60–94% vs fossil fuels, but some lifecycle scenarios show net emissions worse than gasoline; biodiesel cuts particulates ~88% but can raise NOₓ.
- **Land use** — concerns over land grabbing and indirect land-use change (currently <3% of global agricultural land is for biofuel crops).
- **Water** — ~70% of freshwater is used for agriculture; biofuels could overtax water supply/quality; strategic switchgrass planting can reduce nitrate contamination.
- **Biodiversity** — clearing land/forests can disrupt habitats; evaluation metrics still nascent.
- **Fuel performance** — biofuels give favourable **octane** gains but lower fuel economy (ethanol ~25–30% fewer miles/volume vs gasoline); B100 gives ~70% less hydrocarbons, ~50% less particulates/CO, but ~10% more NOₓ.
- **Trade-offs** — advanced biofuels from waste/residue avoid food-fuel conflict and land use, but need heavy investment; algae needs much water; residue removal can deplete soil.
- **Policy and investment** — strongly shaped by national policies (Brazil, China, EU, India, USA) and investment trends (global biofuel investment was ~$3.1 billion in 2015, declining).

## Q15. Write a short note on biodiesel along with its advantages and disadvantages.

**Biodiesel** is an alternative to petroleum-based diesel. Chemically it is a **mixture of monoalkyl esters of fatty acids**, most often obtained from extracted plant oils and/or collected animal fats. Pure vegetable oil cannot be used directly in direct-injection diesel engines (causes engine coking), so oils/fats are converted to biodiesel.

**Advantages** (per USDA, from the notes):
- Renewable; suitable replacement for petroleum diesel; usable in most diesel engines with little/no modification.
- Potential to **reduce GHG emissions**; biodegradable with little/no toxicity; can be made from agricultural or recycled sources.
- Lower CO₂ and **polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (PAH)** emissions; considered **"carbon neutral"** (CO₂ released on burning was captured during crop growth).
- **Higher flash point** than petro-diesel → safer/easier transport and storage.

**Disadvantages:**
- Edible/non-edible oil feedstocks raise **food-vs-fuel competition**, feedstock cost/availability, and land-use-change concerns.
- Can release **higher NOₓ** emissions.
- *(Standard reference)* lower energy content and cold-flow (gelling at low temperature) issues, and oxidative stability concerns.

## Q17. Explain the chemical reaction involved in formation of triglycerides. Enlist at least ten fatty acids present in fats used in formation of biodiesel.

**Formation of triglycerides** *(standard course chemistry — Lecture-16 not in repo):*
A triglyceride is an **ester of glycerol with three fatty acids**, formed by **esterification (condensation)** with the release of three water molecules:

```
   Glycerol  +  3 Fatty acids (RCOOH)  →  Triglyceride  +  3 H₂O
   (C₃H₅(OH)₃)                            (C₃H₅(OOCR)₃)
```
Each –OH of glycerol reacts with the –COOH of a fatty acid to form an ester (–OOC–R) linkage. Vegetable oils and animal fats are mixtures of such triglycerides, which are then **transesterified** to biodiesel.

**Ten fatty acids present in biodiesel fats/oils** *(standard reference):*
1. Palmitic acid (C16:0)
2. Stearic acid (C18:0)
3. Oleic acid (C18:1)
4. Linoleic acid (C18:2)
5. Linolenic acid (C18:3)
6. Myristic acid (C14:0)
7. Lauric acid (C12:0)
8. Palmitoleic acid (C16:1)
9. Arachidic acid (C20:0)
10. Erucic acid (C22:1)
*(also Capric C10:0 and Behenic C22:0)*

## Q18. Describe the chemistry of esterification and transesterification along with its importance in production of biodiesel.

*(Standard course chemistry — Lecture-16 not in repo.)*

**Esterification:** reaction of a **fatty acid (RCOOH)** with an **alcohol (R'OH)**, usually acid-catalysed, to form an **ester + water**. It is used to convert **free fatty acids (FFA)** in low-grade/waste oils into esters (pre-treatment step, reducing soap formation):
```
   RCOOH + R'OH  ⇌  RCOOR' + H₂O   (acid catalyst, e.g. H₂SO₄)
```

**Transesterification (alcoholysis):** the main biodiesel reaction — a **triglyceride reacts with a short-chain alcohol** (usually **methanol**, sometimes ethanol) in the presence of a **base catalyst (NaOH/KOH)** to give **fatty acid methyl esters (FAME = biodiesel)** and **glycerol** as a by-product:
```
   Triglyceride + 3 CH₃OH  ⇌  3 Fatty acid methyl esters (Biodiesel) + Glycerol
                              (NaOH/KOH catalyst)
```

**Importance:** Transesterification **lowers the viscosity** of the oil/fat to make it suitable for diesel engines, produces a fuel with properties close to petro-diesel, and yields valuable **glycerol** as a co-product. Esterification pre-treats high-FFA feedstocks so that base-catalysed transesterification proceeds efficiently without soap formation.

## Q19. Discuss the commercial production of biodiesel with the help of a flow chart. Also explain the chemical reactions involved.

*(Standard course chemistry — Lecture-16 not in repo.)*

**Flow chart:**
```
Oil/Fat feedstock ──► Pre-treatment (degumming, FFA check)
        │
   (if high FFA) ──► Acid esterification (H₂SO₄ + methanol) ──► reduces FFA
        ↓
Transesterification reactor: Oil + Methanol + catalyst (NaOH/KOH), ~60 °C
        ↓
Separation (settling/centrifuge) → two layers
   ├── Upper layer: crude biodiesel (methyl esters)
   └── Lower layer: glycerol (by-product) → glycerol recovery
        ↓
Methanol recovery (distillation, recycled)
        ↓
Biodiesel washing (remove catalyst/soap/methanol) → drying
        ↓
Finished BIODIESEL (FAME)  →  meets ASTM/IS standards
```

**Reactions:**
1. Esterification (FFA pre-treatment): `RCOOH + CH₃OH → RCOOCH₃ + H₂O`
2. Transesterification (main): `Triglyceride + 3 CH₃OH → 3 RCOOCH₃ (biodiesel) + Glycerol`

## Q22. Explain the terms B5, B15, B100. (Refer Lecture-16 — biodiesel.)

Biodiesel is blended with petroleum diesel, and the blend is denoted **"Bxx"**, where **xx = % of biodiesel by volume**:
- **B5** — 5% biodiesel + 95% petro-diesel (usable in conventional diesel engines with no/minimal modification).
- **B15** — 15% biodiesel + 85% petro-diesel.
- **B100** — pure (100%) biodiesel; gives the greatest emission benefits (≈70% less HC, ≈50% less PM/CO) but ~10% more NOₓ, and may need engine considerations and cold-flow management.
