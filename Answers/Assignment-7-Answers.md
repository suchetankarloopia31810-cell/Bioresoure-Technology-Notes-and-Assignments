## Q1. Discuss the significance of pretreatment in bioresource technology and explain the basics of hydrolysis in pretreatment.

**Significance of pretreatment:**
- Lignocellulosic biomass (LCB) is abundantly and evenly distributed globally and is **carbon-neutral**, making it a key renewable source for biofuels and chemicals.
- LCB is composed of **cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin** bound by covalent and hydrogen bonds, making the structure highly **recalcitrant**. Lignin makes the structure resistant to solubilization and inhibits hydrolysis of cellulose/hemicellulose.
- **Pretreatment** breaks these bonds and weakens the association among cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin so the components can be isolated and used. It disrupts the recalcitrant structure → breaks the lignin sheath, degrades hemicellulose, and reduces the crystallinity and degree of polymerization of cellulose.
- It is the **major step** in successful production of valuable products from LCB; success of biofuel/chemical production strongly depends on the pretreatment method. (Pretreatment contributes ≥20% of total conversion cost.)

**Basics of hydrolysis:**
- Hydrolysis is a **double-decomposition reaction with water as one reactant**: `AB + HOH ⇌ AH + BOH`. A covalent bond is broken by inserting a water molecule across it (the opposite of a dehydration-condensation reaction).
- Examples: starch → glucose; natural fats → glycerol + fatty acids; salt → weak acid/base; ester hydrolysis (methyl acetate → acetic acid + methyl alcohol).
- **In bioresource technology:** natural polymers (cellulose, hemicellulose, starch, fats) must be simplified into **simple sugars (glucose, pentose)**, fatty acids, etc., before microbial conversion. Hydrolysis (enzyme, acid or alkali) is used in producing ABE (acetone-butanol-ethanol), bioethanol, bio-ATF and other biofuels. Hydrolysis is divided into physical, chemical, thermochemical, thermophysical and biological types.

## Q3. Discuss the structure and composition of various LCBs.

LCB is mainly composed of three polymers (plus minor proteins, ash, pectin):
- **Cellulose** (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ — typically **30–60%**. A **linear polysaccharide of D-glucose** linked by **β-(1,4)-glycosidic bonds**; crystalline; insoluble in water (soluble in ionic liquids, NMMO). Properties: biocompatibility, stereoregularity, hydrophilicity, reactive hydroxyl groups; basis for fibers, films, composites, fuels and chemicals.
- **Hemicellulose** (C₅H₈O₄)ₘ — typically **20–40%**. Short chains of various polysaccharides (xylan, galactomannan, glucuronoxylan, arabinoxylan, glucomannan, xyloglucan) joined by β-(1,4)/β-(1,3) bonds. **Amorphous, low degree of polymerization → readily degradable** into monosaccharides; used in drug carriers, hydrogels, cosmetics.
- **Lignin** [C₉H₁₀O₃(OCH₃)₀.₉₋₁.₇]ₓ — typically **15–25%**. A complex, 3-D cross-linked aromatic polymer of **phenylpropane units** linked by aryl-ether (β-O-4, α-O-4) and C–C bonds (5-5, β-β). Three basic units: **p-hydroxyphenyl (H), guaiacyl (G), syringyl (S)**. Forms a protective barrier covalently linking cellulose and hemicellulose → main cause of **recalcitrance**.

Crystallized cellulose + hemicellulose matrix is **encrusted by highly polymerized phenolic lignin**, which causes the difficulty in conversion. Composition varies with source (woody vs herbaceous vs agricultural residues).

## Q4. When wood flour is subjected to ultrasonication, how is the disintegration of various components of LCB effected? Sketch the diagram and show it.

**Ultrasonication** works on the principle of **cavitation** using ultrasonic radiation. Cavitation generates **shear forces** that cleave the complex network structure of LCB and promote the extraction of cellulose, hemicellulose and/or lignin.
- It effectively **removes the amorphous hemicellulose and lignin fractions**, which actually **increases the crystallinity** of the remaining cellulose (e.g., in eucalyptus wood, crystallinity rose from ~34.7% to ~35.3–35.5% depending on solvent).
- The choice of solvent (dilute acids/alkalis, organic solvents, ionic liquids) is critical; factors include ultrasound frequency, duration, power and temperature.
- Ultrasound can scale down biomass hydrolysis time **up to 80%**, but it is energy-intensive and prolonged sonication causes particle collision/aggregation.

**Two-step extraction of Cellulose Nanocrystals (CNCs) from wood flour (Figure-13):**
```
   WOOD FLOUR
        │  Step 1: Ethanol + peroxide SOLVOTHERMAL pretreatment
        │          (removes lignin & hemicellulose → purified cellulose)
        ▼
   Delignified cellulose
        │  Step 2: ULTRASONIC DISINTEGRATION (cavitation shear forces)
        ▼
   Cellulose Nanocrystals (CNCs)  — high crystallinity, high thermal stability
```

## Q5. Write short notes on chemical, physical, physicochemical and biological methods of pretreatments.

**Physical:** Prerequisite for other methods; reduces particle size → increases surface area, decreases crystallinity and degree of polymerization. Eco-friendly, no toxic products, but high energy. Methods: **milling** (ball/rod/hammer/vibratory/colloid/wet-disk, ≤0.2 mm), **extrusion** (screws in heated barrel, >300 °C + shear), **microwave** (dielectric polarization → thermal energy, easy/energy-efficient, minimal inhibitors), **ultrasonication** (cavitation shear, cuts hydrolysis time up to 80%).

**Chemical:** Most effective and most used. **Alkali** (NaOH/KOH/lime/ammonia) — saponification cleaves ester linkages between lignin and hemicellulose, solubilizes lignin, swells cellulose; best for low-lignin biomass; drawback: alkali recovery. **Acid** (dilute or concentrated H₂SO₄/HCl/acetic) — solubilizes hemicellulose (removes ~100% with dilute H₂SO₄), disrupts lignin; drawback: inhibitors (furfural, HMF), corrosion. **Ionic liquids (ILs)** — green solvents (negligible vapour pressure, recoverable) that disrupt cellulose H-bonding; drawback: costly and toxic to enzymes. **Organosolv** — organic solvents (ethanol, methanol, acetone) break lignin-hemicellulose bonds, leave pure cellulose, solvents recoverable; drawback: expensive, flammable. **Deep eutectic solvents (DES)** — cheap, biodegradable IL alternatives (e.g., choline chloride + urea/glycerol/acids).

**Physico-chemical (thermochemical):** **Steam explosion** (saturated steam 160–260 °C, 0.69–4.83 MPa, then sudden pressure release → fiber explosion; autohydrolysis by liberated acetic acid; high energy efficiency). **AFEX** (liquid ammonia 1:1, 60–100 °C, high pressure then release; swells lignocellulose, few inhibitors; ammonia recycled). **CO₂ explosion** (supercritical CO₂ penetrates biomass, forms carbonic acid; greener, lower energy). **Liquid hot water** (water 170–230 °C, up to 5 MPa, pH 4–7; hydrolyzes hemicellulose; energy-intensive).

**Biological:** Low-cost, eco-friendly, no inhibitors, low energy; uses lignin-degrading bacteria/fungi (whole cell or enzymes). **White-rot fungi** (e.g., *Phanerochaete chrysosporium*, *Ceriporiopsis subvermispora*) are most effective. Two systems: **hydrolytic** (degrades cellulose/hemicellulose) and **ligninolytic** (depolymerizes lignin). Enzymes: **laccase (Lac), lignin peroxidase (LiP), manganese peroxidase (MnP), versatile peroxidase (VP)**. Drawback: low efficiency, long residence time.

## Q6. How is the position of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin affected in enzyme pretreatment process?

- Lignin is the most abundant aromatic polymer; some fungi, bacteria and insects produce enzymes that digest it. Two families of **ligninolytic enzymes**: **phenol oxidase (laccase, Lac)** and **peroxidases (lignin peroxidase LiP, versatile peroxidase VP, manganese peroxidase MnP)** — heme-containing glycoproteins requiring H₂O₂ as oxidant.
- In enzyme pretreatment, the **ligninolytic system selectively depolymerizes/degrades lignin** while the **hydrolytic system (cellulases, hemicellulases) degrades cellulose and hemicellulose**:
  - **Lignin** — degraded/modified: **LiP** oxidizes non-phenolic lignin; **MnP** oxidizes phenolic lignin (acts in the initial stage); **Lac** oxidizes phenolic (and, with mediators, non-phenolic) substructures. Removal of the lignin barrier exposes the carbohydrates.
  - **Hemicellulose** — degraded by hemicellulolytic enzymes (e.g., xylanases) into C5 sugars (xylose); becomes more accessible once lignin is removed.
  - **Cellulose** — its accessibility/digestibility **increases** as lignin and hemicellulose are removed; cellulolytic enzymes then degrade it to C6 sugars (glucose). White-rot fungi ideally degrade lignin while leaving cellulose relatively intact (selective delignification).
- Net effect: **lignin is removed/modified, hemicellulose is degraded, and cellulose is exposed and made accessible** for enzymatic saccharification and fermentation, increasing the yield of both hydrolysis and fermentation.

## Q7. Explain biomaterials and cite at least ten examples.

**Biomaterial:** a **synthetic (or natural) material used to make devices to replace part of a living system or to function in intimate contact with living tissue**. Biomaterials may be metallic, ceramic, polymeric or composite, designed to **augment, replace or restore** the function of diseased/damaged tissues or organs, and are engineered to interact favorably with biological organisms/molecules. Applications range from load-bearing orthopedic devices to degradable sutures and living skin equivalents; used in tissue engineering, biosensing and drug delivery.
- *(NIH definition, 1983):* any substance (other than a drug), synthetic or natural, used as a whole or part of a system that treats, augments or replaces any tissue, organ or function of the body.

**Examples (≥10):**
1. Hip/joint implants (prostheses)
2. Bone plates / screws / pins
3. Dental implants
4. Contact lenses & intraocular lenses (PMMA)
5. Heart valves
6. Artificial blood vessels
7. Sutures (e.g., poly-glycolic acid)
8. Pacemakers / cardiac assist devices
9. Breast implants
10. Catheters
11. Blood bags, syringes and needles
12. Artificial skin; bone cement

## Q8. Write a short note on the history of biomaterials.

- Biomaterials is a relatively **new field** compared to materials science. Ancient cases exist (glass eyes, metallic/wooden dental implants dating to ancient Egypt).
- The **modern age** could not exist without **aseptic surgical techniques** pioneered by **Sir Joseph Lister** (mid-19th century); the field emerged as a discipline only after the development of **synthetic polymers** before/during/after **World War II**.
- **Before WWII:** implants were mainly **metals** (steel pins, plates, joint replacements).
- **Late 1940s:** **Harold Ridley** observed that **PMMA** shards (from aircraft windshields) in WWII aviators' eyes provoked little inflammatory response → led to **PMMA intraocular lenses** and broader use of polymers as biomaterials.
- **1970s–1980s:** growth of cellular/molecular/developmental biology gave insight into how cells respond to their environment → a **paradigm shift** from inert, nondegradable replacements toward biomaterials that **incorporate biologically active molecules** and interact with biology.
- **Definitions evolved:** Clemson Advisory Board (1974, for WHO) — "systemically pharmacologically inert substance designed for implantation"; Dr. Jonathan Black extended it; **NIH (1983)** gave the modern broad definition. Recent focus is on **polymeric/composite** materials with biologically sensitive properties (publications grew rapidly 1999–2013).

## Q9. Discuss the applications of biomaterials.

Biomaterials are introduced into the body as medical devices. Applications:
- **Medical:** cancer therapy; artificial ligaments and tendons; **orthopedics** (joint replacements, bone plates); **ophthalmic** (contact/intraocular lenses); **wound healing** (surgical sutures, clips); **nerve regeneration**; **reproductive therapy** (breast implants); heart valves, cardiac assist devices, pacemakers; artificial organs (heart, kidney, liver, pancreas, bladder); artificial blood vessels and skin.
- **High-volume products:** blood bags, syringes, needles.
- **Non-medical:** growing cells in culture medium; assay of blood proteins in laboratories.
- Major areas illustrated: **orthopedic, dentistry and ophthalmic**; plus advancing human-origin biomaterials for **therapeutics and tissue engineering**.

## Q10. Define biocompatibility and discuss the requirement for implantation.

**Biocompatibility** is related to the behavior of biomaterials in various environments under different chemical and physical conditions. A material is biocompatible when it exerts the **expected beneficial tissue response and clinically relevant performance**. Other components of biocompatibility: **cytotoxicity, genotoxicity, mutagenicity, carcinogenicity and immunogenicity**.
- The earlier idea that a biocompatible material must be **totally inert** was abandoned — any material always entails some body response, varying with application and patient characteristics (age, sex). So biocompatibility is defined by the various **forms of interaction of the body with the material**, especially at the tissue–material interface.

**Requirements for implantation:** A material must be **biocompatible, biofunctional, bioactive, bioinert and sterilizable** (also not induce inflammation, toxic or allergenic reactions). It must include:
- **Biofunctionality** — perform its designed function for the required time; should not degrade (for long-lasting use) and must keep chemical/mechanical stability (for metals, avoid harmful corrosion).
- **Sterilizability** — must be sterilizable (eliminate impurities/microorganisms) without modifying its properties.
- Body-response classes: **biotolerable** (separated by fibrous tissue — synthetic polymers, most metals), **bioinert** (no chemical reaction — alumina, zirconia, titanium, carbon), **bioactive** (chemical bond with bone, osseointegration — calcium phosphate, hydroxyapatite, bioglass), **biodegradable** (degrade/absorb in the body, no removal surgery — biopolymers).
- *Note:* choice depends on the function and the contact time with the body, combining physical, chemical, mechanical and biological properties.

## Q11. Describe the characteristics and properties of biomaterials.

**Characteristics required** (Figure-9): besides not inducing inflammation/toxic/allergenic reactions, a biomaterial should be **biocompatible, biofunctional, bioactive, bioinert and sterilizable**; sterilization/hygienization must not modify its properties. The surface (directly exposed to the organism) plays a crucial role in biocompatibility. Body-response types: biotolerable, bioinert, bioactive, biodegradable.

**Properties:**
- **Physical properties** — fundamental for cell adhesion; surface properties include **wettability, filler, roughness, softness and chemical composition** (influence cell behavior and cell–biomaterial reactions).
- **Chemical properties** — determine chemical stability and reactivity. The harsh body environment can cause **corrosion/degradation**; corrosion products increase ion concentration → swelling, pain, and may migrate causing adverse reactions, and affect mechanical integrity (premature failure). **Surface functional groups** (–COOH, –OH, –NH₂, –CH₃) affect adsorbed protein and cell/protein interactions.
- **Mechanical properties** — Young's modulus, ductility, tensile/yield/compression strength, fatigue, wear debris. Tissue elasticity varies widely (brain ~0.1–1 kPa, mineralized bone ≥30 kPa). Biomaterials with **Young's modulus close to bone** are preferred (uniform stress distribution, avoid **stress shielding**); high yield/compression strength avoids fractures; ductility aids modeling and dental applications.

## Q12. How is the selection of biomaterials made? Discuss.

The selection process should follow a **logical sequence**:
1. **Analysis of the problem**;
2. **Consideration of requirements**;
3. **Consideration of available materials and their properties**, leading to
4. **Choice of material**.

The choice of a specific biomedical material is determined by:
1. A proper **specification of the desired function** for the material;
2. An accurate **characterization of the environment** in which it must function, and the effects of that environment on the material's properties;
3. A **delineation of the length of time** the material must function;
4. A clear understanding of what is meant by **"safe for human use."**

(Ultimately the choice combines the material's physical, chemical, mechanical and biological properties with the function and contact duration.)

## Q13. Discuss the most common classification of biomaterials.

The most common classes (used singly or in combination) are **polymers, metals, ceramics** (and composites):
- **Polymers** — acrylics, polyamides, polyesters, polyethylene, polysiloxanes, polyurethane, reprocessed biological materials. Examples: EVA membranes (controlled release), poly-glycolic acid (resorbable sutures); applications in artificial heart/kidney/liver/pancreas/bladder, bone cement, catheters, contact lenses, heart valves, joint replacements, pacemaker encapsulation, artificial blood vessels/skin, sutures.
- **Metals** — (a) **316L stainless steel** (iron-base), (b) **titanium and Ti alloys** (Ti-6%Al-4%V; Ti-Ni shape-memory 55%Ni-45%Ti), (c) **cobalt-base alloys** (various Cr-Mo-Ni-W compositions). Most common: 316L stainless steel, Ti-6Al-4V, cobalt-base alloys.
- **Ceramics** — aluminum oxides, calcium phosphates, apatites, graphite, bioglasses. Chosen for inertness, formability, high compressive strength, good wear. Applications: hip prostheses, artificial knees, bone grafts, tissue in-growth (orthopedics, dentistry), heart valves.
- **Composites** — e.g., UHMWPE reinforced with carbon fibers (pyrolyzed acrylic fibers, 6–15 µm, randomly oriented); stronger than single materials; used in dentistry and prostheses (examples: Al₂O₃ on carbon, carbon/PTFE, Al₂O₃/PTFE, PLA-coated carbon fibers).

## Q14. What is biopulping? Discuss its significance.

**Biopulping** is the **fungal pretreatment of wood chips** for the production of mechanical or chemical pulps. Its concept is based on the ability of a restricted number of **white-rot fungi** to **selectively colonize and degrade lignin** in wood, leaving cellulose relatively intact. It is an **environmentally friendly technology** that increases mill throughput or reduces electrical energy consumption.

**Significance:**
- **Energy saving** — softened, porous chips break apart more easily during refining; e.g., MnP treatment cut specific energy consumption ~11% (Scots pine), ~6% (Norway spruce); *Phanerochaete chrysosporium* saves ~30% of fiberizing/refining energy.
- **Reduced pollution / Environmental** — biological process with minimal environmental impact; uses few chemicals; reduces effluent pollution (BOD, COD, AOX, colour) and hazardous gas emissions; consumes pitch → lowers toxicity and BOD of process water.
- **Stronger/better paper** — produces stronger pulp with longer fibers and increased fibrillation; reduces the amount of kraft pulp needed; improves brightness, strength and bleachability; biopulps refine more easily.
- **Chemical pulping benefits** — improved cooking → **reduced kappa number / active alkali charge** and/or reduced cooking time after only 1–2 weeks of treatment; reduced pitch content.
- **Economical** — reduces cooking chemicals and energy; can be implemented in existing plants without major changes; can be done in chip piles or silos.

## Q15. Describe the concerns of the conventional pulping process.

The pulp and paper industry has major impacts on natural ecosystems, air/water quality and human health. Concerns fall under **Resources, Processing, and Disposal**:
- **Impact on forests (Resources):** demand for timber/plant fibre leads to logging of natural forests and intensively managed monoculture plantations (sometimes replacing native forests); new technology opens up even pristine boreal forests; plantations seldom offer the ecological/social benefits of natural forests.
- **Excess use of water:** the industry is a large water user; efforts focus on reducing usage and recirculating/reusing liquid waste streams.
- **Pollutants in water:** main categories are **suspended solids (fibre), BOD, toxicity and colour**. Suspended solids removed by recirculation/clarifier settling (80–90%); BOD reduced by good washing, black-liquor recovery, oxygen delignification and biological treatment; bleaching effluent contains toxic/genotoxic/mutagenic substances — especially **chlorinated organics** (hence shift from chlorine to chlorine dioxide); colour mainly from bleaching (oxygen delignification helps).
- **Atmospheric emissions (Processing):** gaseous (SO₂, NOₓ, chlorine dioxide, **reduced sulphur gases** — the intense kraft-mill odour from H₂S, methyl mercaptan, dimethyl sulphide/disulphide) and particulate (saltcake/Na₂SO₄, lime dust, fly ash). Controlled by scrubbers, incineration in lime kiln, and electrostatic precipitators.
- **Disposal:** consequences of waste-paper disposal. Lignin and degradation products are the most important contributors to environmental pollution.

## Q16. Give an overview of the steps involved in the biopulping process with the help of a simple diagram.

In biopulping, wood chips are **pretreated with fungal inoculum** before refining; the chips become softened and more porous and break apart more easily, saving refining energy.

```
   WOOD CHIPS
        │  1. STEAM decontamination (brief, ~15 s) — kills competing
        │     naturally occurring bacteria/fungi
        ▼
   2. COOL + INOCULATE — spray with dilute inoculum of selected
        │     white-rot fungus (+ aeration/conveyor system)
        ▼
   3. INCUBATE in an AERATED CHIP PILE for ~2 weeks
        │     (warm, moist ~55–60%; fungi colonize chip surface,
        │      hyphae penetrate interior; lignin degraded selectively)
        ▼
   4. SOFTENED, POROUS CHIPS  →  REFINING (mechanical) or COOKING (chemical)
        ▼
   5. Flexible, intact fibers → stronger pulp / paper
```
- *Ceriporiopsis subvermispora* is very competitive on both softwoods and hardwoods; it produces several **MnP and laccase** isoenzymes. Ligninases attack lignin; **xylanases** degrade hemicellulose making pulp permeable for residual-lignin removal. Existing plants need no major new equipment (only asepsis/inoculation/steam delivery).

## Q18. Write a brief note on the constraints of biopulping.

- **No single organism is ideal** for all biopulping applications.
- The chosen organism must accomplish **delignification within a reasonable time**.
- **High moisture content** (~55–60%) must be maintained in chips during biotreatment for optimal fungal colonization and hyphal penetration.
- The **degree of asepsis** must be controlled for successful colonization by the selected strain (resistance to contamination, ability to compete with existing microbial biota).
- There is **no clear correlation** between biopulping efficiency and wood weight/component losses.
- **Enzymes are precise but their action on wood is restricted** by their relatively high molecular weight (though enzymes are more easily applied at mill scale than whole cells).
- Removal of minor wood components (resins, polyphenols/extractives) could relate to biopulping gain.
- Main limitations of the biological pretreatment process: **slow reaction rate and complexity in process control** (and, in general, low efficiency / long residence time).
