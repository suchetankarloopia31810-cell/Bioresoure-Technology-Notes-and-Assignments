## Q1. What is single cell protein (SCP) and why is there a need for it? Discuss its advantages and disadvantages.

**Definition:** Single Cell Protein (SCP) refers to the **dead, dry cells of microorganisms** such as yeast, fungi, bacteria and algae, which are grown on various carbon sources and used for their protein content. The term was first used by **Carol Wilson in 1967**, replacing the less aesthetic terms "petro-protein" and "microbial protein." Because most of the microorganisms used are unicellular, the protein obtained is called "single cell protein." Yeast is the most commonly chosen organism because of its superior nutritional quality. SCP also contains vitamins, fats and minerals.

**Need for SCP:**
- It is an idea to solve **global food/protein scarcity**.
- It relieves the agriculture sector, which uses large land areas for protein crops, while production per unit area is low.
- It is estimated that SCP fermenters covering one-third of a square mile can provide **10% of the world's protein requirement**.
- Agriculture is affected by climate, whereas **SCP technology is independent of climate**.
- The scenario for animal protein is also not encouraging, so SCP is a promising supplement.

**Advantages:**
- High protein content; contains all the essential amino acids.
- Some microorganisms are rich in vitamins.
- High surface-area-to-volume ratio, small doubling time, high growth rate.
- Flexibility in the use of substrate; independent of cultivable land and climate.
- Works on a continuous basis; eco-friendly, cost-effective, energy-efficient.
- Can be genetically controlled.

**Disadvantages / drawbacks:**
- **High nucleic acid content** (18–25 g/100 g protein), whose digestion produces purines → elevated **uric acid** → gout and kidney stones.
- Possible indigestibility of the microbial cell wall.
- Intolerable colour and flavour (especially in algae); possible skin and gastrointestinal reactions (nausea, vomiting).
- Algal SCP may be unsuitable for humans due to high chlorophyll (except *Spirulina*).
- Low density (1–2 g dry weight/litre) and high contamination risk.
- Filamentous fungi grow slowly and some strains produce mycotoxins.

## Q2. What are various suitable substrates for production of SCP? Discuss the sources of microorganisms and the selection criteria for SCP production.

**Substrates suitable for SCP:**
- **Algae:** CO₂ and sunlight are the main growth sources.
- **Fungi:** sulfite waste liquor, prawn shell waste, dairy waste/whey, molasses, and lignocellulosic wastes.
- **Bacteria:** C1–C4 compounds, agricultural/animal/food-processing wastes, methane, methanol, etc.

**Sources of microorganisms:** Algae, fungi, protozoa and bacteria.
- Among algae, *Spirulina* is used most extensively; *Chlorella, Scenedesmus, Dunaliella* on a large scale (problems: foul odour, tastelessness).
- Fungi: *Aspergillus, Fusarium, Candida, Chaetomium, Trichoderma, Penicillium* (wide substrate range, withstand abiotic conditions).
- Bacteria: *Bacillus, Lactobacillus, Pseudomonas, Aeromonas* (success less encouraging).
- **Mixed cultures** show better stability and resistance to contamination.

**Selection criteria:**
- Ability to utilize carbon and nitrogen sources.
- Moderate growth conditions; tolerance to pH, temperature, mineral concentration.
- Resistance against viral infection.
- Non-toxicity and non-pathogenicity.
- Acceptable nutritive value of cell mass.

## Q3. Write a brief note on the nutritional aspects of SCP.

- Nutritional value varies with the microorganism and substrate used.
- Assessed using **amino acid, protein, carbohydrate and lipid profiles, minerals and vitamins**; also palatability, allergies and gastrointestinal effects; long-term feeding, toxicological and carcinogenesis trials.
- Important nutritional indices:
  - **Protein Efficiency Ratio (PER)** = (g weight gained by microorganisms) / (g protein intake by microorganisms)
  - **Biological Value (BV)** = (retained nitrogen) / (absorbed nitrogen)
  - **Net Protein Utilization (NPU)** = (retained nitrogen / nitrogen intake) × 100
  - Also Protein Digestibility Value (PDV).
- **Algae:** rich in proteins (40–60%), fats (5–20%), vitamins A, B, C, D, E; minerals (7%), nucleic acid (4–6%).
- **Fungi:** contain B-complex vitamins; reasonably high amino acid content.
- **Yeast:** ~50% protein and 20% non-protein; contains lysine, thiamine, biotin, riboflavin, niacin, folic acid but **deficient in methionine**. *Aspergillus niger* is well balanced and at par with WHO standards.
- **Bacteria:** crude protein ~80% of dry weight.
- In general, SCP products contain approximately **50–70% crude protein**.

## Q4. Explain the different factors which affect the production of SCP.

SCP production is complex and costly; it is affected by:
1. **Selection of microorganism** — based on growth rate, productivity, yields on low-cost substrates; temperature/pH tolerance; oxygen requirement, heat generation, foaming; growth morphology and genetic stability; ease of recovery and downstream processing; structure and composition of final product (protein content, amino acid profile, RNA level, flavour, aroma, colour, texture).
2. **Safety and acceptability** — most SCP is used as animal feed; products must meet safety requirements; regulatory approval for human consumption is lengthy/expensive.
3. **Nucleic acid content** — fermentation favouring rapid growth raises RNA levels → purines → uric acid → gout/kidney stones; must be controlled.
4. **Digestibility, sensitivity/allergic reactions** to microbial protein.
5. **Absorption of toxic/carcinogenic substances** (e.g., polycyclic aromatic compounds from substrates).
6. **Economic aspects** — fermenter design, heat management, waste disposal, environmental concerns.

## Q5. Discuss single cell protein production processes along with a suitable flow diagram.

Production takes place by **fermentation** using selected strains multiplied on suitable raw materials, followed by separation. Development begins with **microbial screening** (from soil, water, air, swabs), optimization by selection/mutation/genetic methods, determination of cultivation conditions and metabolic pathways, then scale-up with process & apparatus engineering (with attention to energy, cost, safety, environment, and legal/licensing aspects).

**Cultivation methods:**
- **Submerged fermentation:** substrate always in liquid state with nutrients; fermenter operated continuously; biomass harvested continuously, then filtered/centrifuged and dried. Aeration is important; heat is removed by cooling. Yeast/bacteria recovered by **centrifugation**, filamentous fungi by **filtration**.
- **Semisolid fermentation:** substrate used in more solid state (e.g., cassava waste); a special **U-loop fermenter** is used for mass/energy transport.
- **Solid State Fermentation (SSF):** solid substrate (rice/wheat bran) seeded with microorganisms on flatbeds in a temperature-controlled room for several days.

**Main steps:** medium preparation → fermentation → separation → downstream processing.

**Flow (Figure-1):** Raw material/carbon source → medium preparation & sterilization → inoculation → fermentation (with aeration, temperature & pH control) → harvesting (centrifugation/filtration) → drying → SCP product.

## Q6. What are the various steps and technologies applied worldwide for production of SCP? Discuss the Pruteen Technology used.

**Most important steps:**
1. **Selection of microorganism** — fast growing, wide substrate utilization, non-pathogenic, non-toxin producing, high quality and quantity of protein, low nucleic acid.
2. **Selection of substratum** — cheap, readily utilizable, no toxic by-products.
3. **Growing conditions** — ambient conditions, short incubation, simple downstream process.
4. **Post-harvest step** — simple purification and drying without affecting protein quality; supplementation if deficient.

**Technologies used worldwide:** Bel process, **Pekilo** process, Bioprotein process, **Pruteen** process, Quorn production, Symba process, Waterloo process, High-rate algal ponds.

**Pruteen Process:**
- Most adventurous methanol-based process, developed by **Imperial Chemical Industries (ICI), UK, in 1980**.
- Uses the methylotrophic bacterium ***Methylophilus methylotrophus*** to produce feed protein (Pruteen) for chickens and pigs.
- World's largest continuous aerobic bioprocess: a **3000 m³ pressure-cycle airlift fermenter** with inner loop, capable of producing up to **50,000 tonnes** of Pruteen protein.

## Q9. Write a brief note on antibiotics and discuss their top ten classes.

**Antibiotic** literally means "against life." It is a **chemical substance produced by a living organism (usually a microorganism) that is detrimental to other microorganisms.** They are commonly produced by soil microorganisms (bacteria and fungi) and are compounds capable of killing or inhibiting competing microbial species.
- An antibiotic is an antimicrobial substance active against bacteria (antibacterial agent), used to treat/prevent bacterial infections.
- They either kill or inhibit bacterial growth; a few have anti-protozoal activity.
- **Not effective against viruses** (cold, influenza) — those need antivirals.
- Before antibiotics (available from the 1940s), many died of minor infections; afterwards life expectancy rose and surgery became safer.

**Top 10 classes of antibiotics:**
1. Penicillins
2. Tetracyclines
3. Cephalosporins
4. Quinolones
5. Lincomycins
6. Macrolides
7. Sulfonamides
8. Glycopeptides
9. Aminoglycosides
10. Carbapenems

## Q10. Explain the different schemes for classification of antibiotics with suitable examples.

Antibiotics are classified by several schemes:

1. **According to Spectrum of Activity:**
   - **Narrow/short spectrum** — target only specific bacteria (e.g., penicillin G, vancomycin).
   - **Broad/wide spectrum** — act on many kinds of bacteria (e.g., tetracycline, chloramphenicol).
2. **According to Mode of Action:**
   - **Bacteriostatic** — inhibit growth without killing (e.g., tetracycline).
   - **Bactericidal** — kill bacteria (e.g., penicillin, cephalosporin).
3. **According to Mechanism of Action:** (1) inhibit cell-wall synthesis (e.g., penicillin), (2) interfere with cell-membrane integrity, (3) inhibit nucleic acid synthesis, (4) inhibit protein synthesis, (5) inhibit metabolic pathways.
4. **According to Chemical Structure:** e.g., **beta-lactams** (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams), quinolones, sulphonamides.
5. **According to Absorbability.**

## Q11. Explain the chemical structure of antibiotics with the help of cephalosporin and penicillin.

Both belong to the **beta-lactam group** — they share a **beta-lactam ring** in common.

- **Cephalosporin:** general structure contains the beta-lactam ring with two significant side groups **R1 and R2**. By changing R1 and R2, various types of cephalosporins are obtained.
- **Penicillin:** basic structure is **6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA)** combined with an **N-acyl group** (side chain). Structurally it is a combination of an **acyl side chain + beta-lactam ring (in the middle) + thiazolidine ring**. The beta-lactam ring (penicillin nucleus) is the chief requirement for biological activity, while the side-chain structures (R₁, R₂) determine antibacterial and pharmacological properties.

## Q12. What is penicillin? Discuss the history behind the discovery and production.

**Penicillins** are a group of antibiotics originally derived from *Penicillium* moulds. They are **bactericidal** antibacterial drugs that work against a wide range of bacteria by **interfering with bacterial cell-wall synthesis**, and they belong to the beta-lactam group. Examples: penicillin G, penicillin V, procaine penicillin, benzathine penicillin.

**History:**
- Discovered in **1928 by Alexander Fleming** (Professor of Bacteriology, St. Mary's Hospital, London) — the first antibiotic.
- Discovery was **accidental**: returning from holiday (Sept 3, 1928), Fleming found a *Staphylococcus* petri dish accidentally left open and contaminated by a mould; around the mould blob, bacterial growth was inhibited. The mould was identified as ***Penicillium notatum***.
- Fleming found the "mould juice" killed many harmful bacteria (streptococcus, meningococcus, diphtheria bacillus).
- In **1939**, **Howard Florey and Ernst Chain** purified penicillin into powder; in **1941** they treated a patient; in **1943** they achieved large-scale production — helping WWII casualties.
- Fleming, Chain and Florey received the **Nobel Prize in Medicine & Physiology in 1945**.

## Q13. Explain the general structure of penicillin and discuss the significance of R and M groups in the side chains.

**General structure:** Penicillins contain **6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA)** with an **acyl (R) side chain** attached; structurally = acyl side chain + beta-lactam ring + thiazolidine ring. The cation/metal **M** is associated with the carboxyl group.

**Significance of R (side chain):**
- Variation of R gives different penicillins with different designations and potencies. Examples (UK/USA designation):
  - 2-Pentenyl (I/F), n-Amyl (Dihydro), **Benzyl (II/G = Penicillin G)**, p-Hydroxybenzyl (III/X), n-Heptyl (IV/K), **Phenoxymethyl (V/Vee = Penicillin V)**.
- The R group (via its precursor) controls the type and properties (acid stability, β-lactamase resistance, antimicrobial spectrum).

**Significance of M (in the main ring / cation):** imparts **solubility and ingestion-rate control**:
- **Direct rapid-action injection:** M = Na or K
- **Oral tablets:** M = K, Ca, Al
- **Delayed-action oil-based injection:** M = procaine or other derivative (limited water solubility).

*Note:* Penicillin is measured in **units** — 1 unit of penicillin G = activity in 0.6 µg of sodium penicillin, so 1 mg sodium penicillin = 1667 units.

## Q14. What is an ideal penicillin? Discuss its classification based on the mode of production. Also describe the side effects.

**Ideal penicillin (properties):**
- Kills pathogenic bacteria causing infection.
- Causes no allergy to the host/patient.
- Causes no damage/harm to host cells.
- Stable when stored in liquid or solid form.

**Classification based on mode of production:**
1. **Natural penicillins** — produced/harvested naturally by the mould through fermentation; first used clinically; inhibit cell-wall synthesis. Example: **Penicillin G (benzyl penicillin)** — adding phenoxyacetic acid only enhances yield.
2. **Biosynthetic penicillins** — synthesized by the mould only when a precursor is added. Example: **Penicillin V (phenoxymethyl penicillin)** — produced when phenoxyacetic acid is added during fermentation.
3. **Semisynthetic penicillins** — cannot be made by fermentation; the side chain is **chemically attached to 6-APA** (or by enzymatic synthesis). They have improved coverage/effectiveness (streptococci, staphylococci, gram-negative and anaerobic organisms), can be given orally or parenterally, and prevent penicillins from becoming obsolete. Examples: amoxicillin, ampicillin, azidocillin, azlocillin.

**Side effects of penicillin:**
- Watery or bloody diarrhoea; fever, chills, body aches, flu symptoms.
- Easy bruising/bleeding, unusual weakness; reduced or no urination.
- Severe skin rash, itching or peeling.
- Agitation, confusion, unusual thoughts/behaviour, or seizures (black-out/convulsions).

## Q16. What is antibiotic resistance and what is the opinion of WHO on it?

**Antibiotic resistance** occurs when bacteria change in a way that **reduces or eliminates the effectiveness of drugs/chemicals/agents** designed to cure or prevent infection. The bacteria survive and multiply, causing more harm — it is the ability of microbes to resist medication that once successfully treated the patient.

**WHO's opinion on antibiotic resistance:**
- It is **one of the biggest threats to global health, food security and development today**.
- It can affect anyone, of any age, in any country.
- It occurs naturally, but **misuse of antibiotics in humans and animals accelerates** it.
- A growing number of infections (pneumonia, tuberculosis, gonorrhoea, salmonellosis) are becoming harder to treat.
- It leads to **longer hospital stays, higher medical costs and increased mortality**.

## Q17. Discuss large-scale commercial production of penicillin G with the help of a suitable flow chart.

Commercial production of Penicillin G is carried out by **aerobic batch (submerged) fermentation** using *Penicillium chrysogenum* (note in the lecture spelled "crysogennum"):

1. **Inoculum build-up:** Culture on agar slant → aseptically to shake flask (suspension) → seed transfer vessel → seed tank (transfer vessel), maintaining desired physicochemical conditions.
2. **Fermentation medium:** maize (corn) steep liquor base diluted to 4–5% solids, fortified with 4–5% lactose solids and nutrients (magnesium, zinc and/or sodium sulfate, ammonium acetate, potassium dihydrogen phosphate <0.2%). **CaCO₃** added to adjust pH to 5–6.
3. **Precursor addition:** organic precursors control yield/type — **phenylacetic acid** precursor for Type-II penicillin (Penicillin G).
4. **Sterilization:** moist-heat sterilization at **115 °C for ½ hour**, cooled to 20 °C, then inoculated with *P. chrysogenum*.
5. **Fermentation:** sterile air blown for **4–5 days** at controlled temperature (±½ °C); yield maximized by periodic assay of broth.
6. **Mould separation:** by **vacuum rotary filter** (separated mould used as food supplement).
7. **Extraction:** from mycelia-free broth by solvent extraction; continuous filtration at 1–2 °C; filtrate/beer adjusted to **pH 2.5** with 10% H₃PO₄; penicillin salt extracted with **amyl acetate**.
8. **Buffering/recovery:** raffinate (water phase) → solvent recovery still; extract buffered with sodium salt solution to **pH 7.5**; crude penicillin returns to aqueous phase; mixture centrifuged, solvent recirculated.
9. **Purification:** second acidic extraction, reversion to pyrogen-free distilled water with alkaline salt (Na, K, Ca, or Al); super-centrifuge separation; pressurized through biological filter (removes bacteria/pyrogens); concentrated by **freeze drying or vacuum spray drying**.
10. **Crystallization:** crystalline penicillin salts obtained by salting-out with a neutral salt of the desired cation; purified by recrystallization from organic solvents.
11. **Procaine penicillin:** oil-soluble; made by reacting penicillin concentrate (20–30%) with 50% aqueous procaine hydrochloride.

(Flow chart references OUR = oxygen uptake rate, OTR = oxygen transfer rate, MCB = master cell bank, MWCB = manufacturer's working cell bank.)

## Q18. Draw and discuss the schematic diagram for batch production of penicillin.

The **batch (aerobic submerged) fermentation** scheme:

```
Agar slant culture (P. chrysogenum)
        ↓ (aseptic transfer)
Shake flask (culture suspension)
        ↓
Seed transfer vessel  →  Seed tank (transfer vessel)
        ↓
MAIN FERMENTATION VESSEL (Bioreactor)
  - Medium: corn steep liquor (4–5% solids) + 4–5% lactose + nutrients + CaCO₃ (pH 5–6)
  - Precursor: phenylacetic acid (for Penicillin G)
  - Sterilized at 115 °C / ½ h, cooled to 20 °C, inoculated
  - Sterile air, 4–5 days, temp ±½ °C
        ↓
Vacuum rotary filter  →  mould removed (used as feed supplement)
        ↓ (mycelia-free broth/beer)
Solvent extraction (pH 2.5 with H₃PO₄; amyl acetate)
        ↓
Buffering with Na-salt (pH 7.5) → centrifuge → solvent recycle
        ↓
Purification (2nd acidic extraction → pyrogen-free water + alkaline salt)
        ↓
Super-centrifuge → biological filter → freeze/spray drying
        ↓
Crystallization / recrystallization
        ↓
Penicillin salt (Na, K, Ca, Al) / Procaine penicillin
```

**Significance of precursors in penicillin biosynthesis:** Precursors (and hence the side chains) strongly influence penicillin properties. Without a side-chain precursor, only **benzyl penicillin** can be isolated from the natural penicillins formed. Adding a suitable precursor gives the desired (often **semisynthetic**) penicillin. Penicillin G and V are produced commercially. Compared to natural penicillins, semisynthetic penicillins have **improved acid stability, resistance to β-lactamases, and a broader antimicrobial spectrum**.
