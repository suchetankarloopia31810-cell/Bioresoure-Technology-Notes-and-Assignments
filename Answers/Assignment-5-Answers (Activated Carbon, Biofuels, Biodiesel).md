## Q1. Write a brief note on activated carbon.

Activated carbon (also **activated charcoal**) is a form of carbon processed to have **small, low-volume pores** that increase the **surface area** available for adsorption or chemical reactions. It has a **high degree of microporosity**. The surface area varies greatly with the precursor (raw material) and carbonization conditions; reported values are about **1500–3000 m²/g** (by gas adsorption). Chemical treatment enhances adsorption properties. When derived from coal it is "activated coal," from coke "activated coke," and from charcoal "activated carbon" — all performing the same function. It is prepared by **chemical or physical activation and microwave radiation methods**, used as an **adsorbent** by separation and purification industries. It is a microporous, homogeneous structure with high surface area and radiation stability; adsorption capacity depends on **porosity and surface chemistry**. Available in **powder, granules and pellets**.

## Q2. Why is there a need for switching from traditional feedstock (fossil origin) to bioresources for production of activated carbon?

- Traditional AC was made from **coal, lignite, peat, petroleum residue, wood** — which are **expensive and exhaustible**.
- Bioresources (agricultural, municipal, industrial and forestry bio-wastes) are **cheap, easily available, renewable and ecologically suitable**.
- Carbon prepared from biomass shows **excellent surface properties, high porosity and high specific surface area**.
- Fossil feedstocks face **environmental concerns, high cost and non-renewability** → cannot be a permanent choice.
- Bioresource feedstock is logically more acceptable due to **low cost, ease of availability and environmental compatibility**, contributing to environmental sustainability and future commercial benefit. The cost of AC from bioresources is much lower than commercial AC.

## Q3. Discuss in short the various biomass feedstocks for activated carbon.

Biomass covers all organic material (living plants, plant waste, animal waste). Categories:
- **Dedicated energy crops:** grown on less-arid, less-fertile land; two types — **herbaceous** (bamboo, wheatgrass, sorghum) and **woody** (poplar, willow, maple, walnut).
- **Forest residues:** from logging timber or excess biomass in forests (reduces fire/pest risk, improves vitality).
- **Agricultural crop residues:** wheat, barley, rye, oat, maize, rice, rapeseed, sunflower — abundant, diverse, widely distributed.
- **Algae:** microalgae, seaweed, cyanobacteria; grow in fresh, saline, brackish or wastewater.
- **Municipal Solid Waste (MSW):** household wastes — food waste, paper, wood trimmings, textiles.
- **Wet waste:** food wastes, sewage sludge, manure slurries, organic industrial wastes, biogas.

Agricultural wastes used: palm shells, fruit shell, groundnut shell, coconut shell, *Ricinus communis* seed shell; plant parts (root, stem, bark, flower, leaf, fruit peel, husk, shell, stone). Classified as **woody** (cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin) and **non-woody** (also lipids, protein, sugar, starch, etc.).

**Selection criteria:** high carbon content; low inorganic matter (low ash); high density & volatile matter; abundant/low cost; potential extent of activation; low degradation on storage; high % yield.

## Q4. What are the different types of activated carbon? How is the structure explained?

**Main types:**
1. **Powdered Activated Carbon (PAC)** — particle size ~5–150 Å; used in liquid-phase adsorption; reduced cost, operational flexibility.
2. **Granular Activated Carbon (GAC)** — 0.2–5 mm; gas & liquid phase; clean handling, longer life, improved hardness, regenerable.
3. **Extruded Activated Carbon (EAC)** — cylindrical pellets 1–5 mm; gas-phase, heavy-duty.

**Additional types:** Bead Activated Carbon (BAC, from petroleum pitch); Impregnated Carbon (chemisorption/bacteriostatic); Polymer-Coated Carbon (for hemoperfusion); Activated Carbon Cloth (ACC); Activated Carbon Fibers (ACF).

**Structure:** X-ray diffraction shows it is similar to **graphite** — layers of fused hexagons ~0.34 nm apart held by Van der Waals forces (ideal graphite = **ABAB lattice**). Activated carbon is made of **tiny graphite-like platelets** (few atoms thick, 20–100 Å diameter) forming walls of molecular-sized cavities (pore system). Hexagonal rings are **randomly oriented** (many cleaved), so the structure is highly **disordered/"turbostratic"** with larger inter-layer separation (~3.60 Å). Edges carry **oxygen-containing surface functional groups** (carboxylic acid, phenolic hydroxyl, quinone carbonyl, lactones, anhydrides, peroxides).

## Q5. Briefly summarize the properties of activated carbon. How are the pores so important?

**Physicochemical properties (Table-1):** *Physical* — particle density, bulk density, pore volume, ball-pan hardness, particle size distribution, ash content, moisture content, carbon content. *Chemical/adsorption* — surface area, iodine number, carbon tetrachloride number, benzene number.

Key properties: **Pore structure**; **Hardness/Abrasion** (coconut-shell AC has the highest hardness); **Adsorptive properties** (driven by **London dispersion forces** — additive, non-specific, temperature-independent, short-ranged); **Carbon Tetrachloride Activity (CTC)**; **Apparent/bulk density**; **Moisture** (ideally 3–6%); **Ash content** (as low as possible — quality increases as ash decreases); **pH** (coconut-shell AC: pH 9–11); **Particle size**; **Iodine number** (indicates surface area, used as QC parameter); **Surface area** (by **BET** method, up to ~1500 m²/g).

**Importance of pores:** AC purifies primarily by **adsorption** (molecules trapped by internal/external surface). Per IUPAC, pores are classified by size:
- **Macropores:** > 50 nm
- **Mesopores:** 2–50 nm
- **Micropores:** < 2 nm

**Micropores contribute the major part of the internal surface area**; macro- and mesopores act as "highways" into the carbon particle and are crucial for **kinetics**. Pore volume is generally > 0.2 mL/g and internal surface area > 400 m²/g (N₂ BET). *(Reproduce Figure-6: pore structure.)*

## Q6. Describe the process for production of activated carbon with the help of a suitable flow diagram.

The most important step is **activation** — converting the raw/carbonized material into a finely crystalline carbon permeated by randomly distributed pores, giving extended internal surface area (pulverized charcoal external area is only 2–4 m²/g vs ~1000 m²/g for AC). Two main processes:

1. **Carbonization:** thermal decomposition of bioresource in an **inert environment at < 800 °C**; a pyrolytic reaction (dehydrogenation, condensation, hydrogen transfer, isomerization); removes O, H, N, S, leaving **biochar**.
2. **Activation:** oxidizing the char at **800–900 °C** in presence of air, CO₂ or steam to develop pore structure. Two main routes: **wet-chemical process** (single-stage, uses chemicals) and **physical/thermal-gas process** (two-stage, uses gases/steam).

**General flow (Figure-7):** raw bioresource (coconut shells, stones, wood, sawdust, husks, etc.) → cleaning, sizing, upstream processing → impregnation with reagent-1 → carbonization (controlled conditions → porous charcoal) → washing & drying → impregnation with reagent-2 → activation (physical/chemical) → washing & drying → **activated carbon** ready for use.

## Q7. Depict various possible routes for synthesis of active carbon from biomass with the help of a diagram.

Any biomass with high carbon proportion is an ideal precursor (olive stones, rice husk, coconut husk, bamboo; industrial wastes like lignin, tara gum, sawdust). *(Reproduce Figure-9: synthesis routes.)*
- **Biochar** is prepared by **carbonization**.
- **Activated carbons** are obtained in two well-known ways — **physical activation** and **chemical activation** — giving more developed porosity.
- **Non-conventional methods:** **microwave heating** and **hydrothermal carbonization** (for energy/chemical savings).

Porosity, surface chemistry and yields depend strongly on starting biomass composition and operating conditions.

**Example (rice husk, RH):**
- **Physical activation:** carbonization (500–900 °C, N₂/Ar) then activation by CO₂/steam (800–1000 °C). CO₂ creates pore openings → larger micropore volume but narrower distribution; steam widens micropores → wider distribution (micro + meso). CO₂ preferred (clean, easy to handle, controllable).
- **Chemical activation:** carbonization + activation in a single step using KOH, NaOH, H₃PO₄, ZnCl₂; pyrolysis at 400–900 °C in absence of air; inhibits tar formation → better yield. Impregnation ratio (0.25–4) and activation temperature strongly affect yield and pore characteristics.
- **Self activation:** uses gases emitted during pyrolysis to activate carbon (saves activating agent cost; ideal temperature for kenaf core 970–1090 °C).

## Q8. What are the different applications of activated carbon? Discuss the factors affecting production.

**Applications:** metals recovery (gold, silver); food & beverage (decaffeination, removal of odour/taste/colour); medicinal (treating poisonings/ailments); **air-emission purification** (VOCs, odours); **biogas purification** (removing H₂S, siloxanes, terpenes); **remediation** of contaminated soil/groundwater; **chemicals purification**; **wastewater treatment** (COD, BOD, TOC, BTEX, hydrocarbons, AOX, pesticides, cyanides, odour, colour). Used in petroleum, fertilizer, nuclear, pharmaceutical, cosmetic, textile, automobile and vacuum industries; also as a **catalyst in biodiesel production**.

**Factors affecting AC production:**
1. **Raw materials** — high carbon, low inorganic/ash, potential extent of activation, low degradation on storage, high density & volatile content, stable supply, inexpensive. (Lignocellulosic materials ≈ 45% of raw materials; coconut shell & fruit stones most common.)
2. **Temperature** — activation usually > 800 °C in steam/CO₂; higher temperature increases BET surface area but reduces yield (more volatiles released, more fixed carbon and ash).
3. **Activation time** — typically 1–3 h; longer time increases BET surface area but lowers % yield.
4. **Type and concentration of chemicals** used in impregnation/chemical activation.

*(Bonus — Reactivation/Regeneration: restoring adsorptive capacity by desorbing adsorbed contaminants, usually by thermal reactivation in a rotary kiln — drying ~105 °C, desorption/decomposition 500–900 °C under inert atmosphere, residual organic gasification by steam/CO₂ at ~800 °C; other methods: chemical/solvent, microbial, electrochemical, ultrasonic, wet-air oxidation.)*

## Q9. Write a brief note on biofuels. Discuss the relevance of biofuel technology for developing countries like India.

**Biofuel** is a fuel (gas, liquid or solid) derived from **biomass** (plant/algae material or animal waste). Since feedstock can be replenished, it is a **renewable energy** source, unlike fossil fuels. It is advocated as a **cost-effective and environmentally benign alternative** to petroleum (especially amid rising oil prices and global-warming concerns). Crops used are high in sugar (sugarcane, sugarbeet, sweet sorghum), starch (maize, tapioca) or oils (soybean, rapeseed, coconut, sunflower). Biofuels include liquid fuels (ethanol, methanol, biodiesel, Fischer–Tropsch diesel) and gaseous fuels (hydrogen, methane). **Biggest difference from petroleum: oxygen content** (biofuels 10–45%, petroleum essentially none); biofuels also have very low sulfur.

**Relevance (especially for developing countries like India):**
- **Energy security**, environmental concerns, foreign-exchange savings, and rural-sector socioeconomic benefits.
- Biomass-based fuel technology can **employ more people** than fossil-fuel technology (widespread biomass availability).
- Helps the transition from a **hydrocarbon economy to a carbohydrate economy**.
- Advantages: easily available from common biomass; CO₂ cycle in combustion; environmentally friendly; biodegradable; contributes to sustainability; produces equivalent GDP growth, reduces oil imports and fossil energy intensity.

## Q10. Discuss the significance of Fischer–Tropsch (FT) synthesis technology for biofuel production.

The **Fischer–Tropsch (FT) process** is a collection of chemical reactions that converts a mixture of **carbon monoxide and hydrogen (syngas)** into **liquid hydrocarbons**, over metal catalysts (typically **Ru, Co, Fe**) at **150–300 °C** and 1 to tens of atmospheres. Developed by **Franz Fischer and Hans Tropsch in Germany (1925)**.

**Significance for biofuels:**
- Syngas from **biomass gasification** (including lignocellulosic residues) becomes the feedstock for conversion into biofuels via FT — yielding **biosyngas-derived synthetic fuel** to replace fossil fuels.
- Lignocellulosic material is a **low-cost feedstock** for large-scale liquid biofuel.
- Temperature, pressure and catalyst behaviour influence FT activity; catalyst factors determine CO conversion and selectivity to **C5+ hydrocarbons**.
- Products are mainly **aliphatic straight-chain hydrocarbons** (plus branched/unsaturated hydrocarbons and primary alcohols): CH₄, C₂H₄, C₂H₆, LPG (C3–C4), gasoline (C5–C12), diesel (C13–C22), waxes (C23–C33). Distribution depends on catalyst and process parameters (temperature, pressure, residence time). It enables clean fuel from renewable feedstock by integrating biomass gasification with FTS.

## Q13. How are biofuels classified on the basis of feedstock? Discuss them.

**FAO (USA) classification:** wood fuels, agro-fuels, municipal by-products (based on biofuel sources).

**Broad classification:** *(Reproduce Figure-8.)*
- **Primary biofuels:** natural, **unprocessed** biomass (firewood, wood chips, pellets) used directly for heating, cooking, electricity.
- **Secondary biofuels:** produced by **processing** biomass (solids like charcoal; liquids like ethanol, biodiesel, bio-oil; gases). Sub-divided by **generation**:
  - **First generation (1G):** from **food** feedstocks (sugar, starch, vegetable oil, animal fat) — bioethanol, biodiesel, bioalcohols, biogas, syngas. Causes food-chain/land-use conflicts.
  - **Second generation (2G):** from **non-food crops / waste biomass** (cellulosic biomass, wheat/corn stalks, wood, waste vegetable oil); "advanced biofuels"; need different technology.
  - **Third generation (3G):** from **algae** ("oilgae"); higher yields (up to ~30× per unit area) with lower inputs.
  - **Fourth generation (4G):** from **engineered/metabolically-engineered algae** ("algae-to-biofuels") to reduce GHG further.

## Q14. What are the various key issues and performance considerations with respect to biofuel sustainability?

- **Food–fuel debate:** competition for cropland; biofuels estimated to contribute **20–40%** of food-price rise during 2006–2011 (along with oil prices, weather, speculation).
- **Emissions:** results vary widely — biofuels can reduce GHG by **60–94%** vs fossil fuels, but in some lifecycle scenarios can be worse than gasoline; biodiesel cuts particulates ~88% but raises NOx.
- **Land:** <3% of global agricultural land currently used for biofuel crops; concerns over land-grabbing and indirect land-use change.
- **Water:** ~70% of world freshwater used in agriculture; biofuels may overtax supply and quality.
- **Biodiversity:** land/forest clearing can disrupt habitats.
- **Fuel performance:** biofuels give **higher octane** but lower energy economy (ethanol = 25–30% fewer miles per volume than gasoline); biodiesel cuts hydrocarbons/particulates/CO but raises NOx (~10%) and acetaldehyde; negligible sulfur oxides.
- **Trade-offs:** advanced biofuels from waste/residues avoid food-fuel conflict and reduce land/water pressure but need heavy investment and cost reduction; algae has high water needs and costly dewatering step.

## Q15. Write a short note on biodiesel along with its advantages and disadvantages.

**Biodiesel** is a **liquid biofuel** obtained by chemical processes from **vegetable oils or animal fats and an alcohol**, usable in diesel engines (alone or blended). **ASTM** defines it as a mixture of **long-chain monoalkyl esters of fatty acids** from renewable resources. Blends are denoted **"Bx"** (x = % biodiesel; B100 = pure). It has a higher cetane number than diesel, no aromatics, no sulfur, and ~10–11% oxygen by weight → lower CO, HC and PM emissions.

**Advantages:** renewable; low toxicity; biodegrades faster than diesel; lower emissions (CO, PM, PAHs, aldehydes); lower health risk; no SO₂; **higher flash point (≥100 °C)** → safer transport; blendable in any proportion; excellent lubricant; usable in conventional diesel engines without modification; can use used cooking oils/fat residues.

**Disadvantages:** slightly higher fuel consumption (lower calorific value); slightly higher NOx; higher freezing point (inconvenient in cold climates); less stable (no long-term storage > 6 months); may degrade plastic/rubber gaskets in pure form (use Teflon); dissolves tank deposits which flush into engine (clean tanks first). *These are reduced when used in blends with diesel.*

## Q17. Explain the chemical reaction involved in formation of triglycerides. Enlist at least ten fatty acids present in fats used in biodiesel.

- Oils and fats (lipids) are **fatty glycerol esters = triglycerides**. *(Reproduce Figure-20.)*
- **Formation:** a triglyceride is formed by **condensation of one glycerol molecule with three fatty acids** (R′, R″, R‴ are hydrocarbon chains, usually 12–18 carbons), releasing water:

  glycerol + 3 fatty acids → triglyceride + 3 H₂O

- The three chains may be of equal/different length and differ in the number of **double bonds**. Fatty acids may be **saturated (SFA)** (only single bonds) or **unsaturated (NSFA)**.
- Notation **x:y** = number of carbons (x) and number of double bonds (y); y = 0 for all SFAs.

**Ten important fatty acids** (from Table-2): lauric, myristic, palmitic, stearic, oleic, linoleic, linolenic, ricinoleic, arachidic, palmitoleic (with caprylic/capric also). The most frequent are **lauric, palmitic, stearic, linoleic and linolenic**. (E.g., ricinoleic dominates castor oil, oleic in olive oil, linoleic in soybean oil, linolenic in linseed oil.)

## Q18. Describe the chemistry of esterification and transesterification along with its importance in biodiesel production.

**Esterification:** a chemical reaction in which two reactants (typically an **alcohol + an acid**) form an **ester** as product:

  CH₃COOH + CH₃CH₂OH → CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ + H₂O

**Transesterification:** the process of **exchanging the organic group R″ of an ester with the organic group R′ of an alcohol**, catalyzed by acid, base, or enzymes (lipases). Biodiesel is produced by transesterification of vegetable oil/animal fat with an alcohol:
- With **methanol** → mixture of **methyl esters (FAME)**.
- With **ethanol** → mixture of **ethyl esters**.
- **Glycerin** is the by-product (co-product) in both cases.
- Stoichiometry: **1 mol oil + 3 mol alcohol → 3 mol esters + 1 mol glycerin** (reversible; excess alcohol shifts equilibrium right — usually 100% excess = 6 mol alcohol/mol oil).

**Importance:** transesterification is the **most important step** in biodiesel production — it converts the viscous triglyceride into the lighter mixture of esters (biodiesel) whose properties match diesel. Acidic esterification is used as a **preliminary stage** for highly acidic raw materials (FFA reduced to < 2%) before basic transesterification (to avoid soap formation).

## Q19. Discuss the commercial production of biodiesel with the help of a flow chart. Also explain the chemical reactions involved.

**Stages (Box-3):** (a) treatment of raw materials, (b) alcohol-catalyst mixing, (c) chemical reaction, (d) separation of products, (e) purification of products. *(Reproduce Figures-27/28/29 flow charts.)*

1. **Treatment of raw materials:** control free fatty acids (FFA < 2% for basic catalysis), water and non-saponifiables; high-FFA feedstock needs acid esterification first.
2. **Alcohol–catalyst mixing:** alcohol (anhydrous) mixed with catalyst (NaOH/KOH, or Na/K methoxide) until dissolved; alcohol:oil = 6:1 molar (R = 0.25, i.e. ~100% excess).
3. **Chemical reaction (transesterification):** oil + alkoxide reacted at **50–60 °C** under stirring (methanol b.p. ~68 °C); reaction proceeds in **three steps**: TG → DG → MG → glycerin, each releasing a methyl ester (ME):
   - TG + MOH → DG + ME
   - DG + MOH → MG + ME
   - MG + MOH → G + ME
4. **Separation:** FAME and glycerin separate by **decantation** (different densities) or **centrifugation** (faster); catalyst & excess alcohol concentrate in glycerin phase.
5. **Purification:** FAME washed (first with acidified water to neutralize, then water), neutralized and **dried**; glycerin purified (methanol recovered) for large-scale plants.

**Key reactions:** transesterification of triglyceride with methanol → FAME + glycerin (base-catalyzed); for high-FFA feed, acid esterification of FFA with methanol → ester + water (preliminary).

## Q21. Make a comparison between biodiesel and petrodiesel with respect to physical properties and emission.

*(Reproduce Table-6 — physical properties; Table-7 — emissions, in %.)* Salient points:

**Physical properties:** Biodiesel has a **higher cetane number**, **higher flash point** (safer to handle/transport), **higher freezing/cloud/pour point**, slightly **lower calorific value** (higher fuel consumption), contains **~10–11% oxygen**, **no sulfur and no aromatics**, and is an **excellent lubricant** (restores lubricity to low-sulfur diesel). ## Q22. Explain the terms B5, B15, B100 (refer to Lecture-16 Q&A).

Biodiesel blends with conventional diesel are denoted **"Bx"**, where **x = percentage of biodiesel** in the blend (rest is petroleum diesel):
- **B5** = 5% biodiesel + 95% diesel.
- **B15** = 15% biodiesel + 85% diesel.
- **B100** = 100% (pure) biodiesel.

Lower blends (B5–B20) can be used in conventional diesel engines **without modification** and avoid the need for separate, costly storage infrastructure; they also reduce the disadvantages of pure biodiesel. Higher blends (up to B100) give the greatest emission benefits but may pose cold-flow and material-compatibility issues. As per the PCRA Q&A in the notes, biodiesel for India is best made from **non-edible oils (Jatropha, Karanja/Karanjia, rice bran)** grown on wasteland (~80 million hectares available), giving rural employment; it is mostly used as **10–20% blends**, and the fuel must meet **ASTM/DIN specifications**. It is also significant for **Indian Railways** (largest diesel user) — biodiesel at 1–2% retains the lubricity of low-sulfur diesel.
